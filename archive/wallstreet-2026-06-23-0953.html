The Lead

Stocks tumble at the open as a global chip rout deepens — Nasdaq off more than 2%, Micron down ~12% (as of ~9:40 AM ET)

U.S. stocks fell sharply at Tuesday's open as a worldwide technology selloff that began Monday accelerated, with memory-chip shares routed on fresh doubts about stretched AI valuations. Shortly after the bell the Nasdaq Composite was down about 2.3%, the S&P 500 about 1.5% and the Dow Jones Industrial Average about 0.6% — the blue-chip index holding up better as the damage concentrated in semiconductors. Micron sank roughly 12% at the open ahead of its earnings Wednesday, SanDisk fell about 11% and Seagate dropped more than 7%. The selling spread from Asia, where South Korea's Kospi plunged 9.99% — its steepest drop in more than three months — triggering a 20-minute trading halt as Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix each tumbled more than 12%, and Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.55%.

Megacap names compounded the pressure. Oracle slipped about 2.6% to $170.85 after disclosing it cut roughly 21,000 jobs over the past year as it retools around AI, and Alphabet — which suffered its worst day in over a year Monday, down about 5% on AI-capex worries and high-profile DeepMind talent departures — stayed under pressure. Newly public SpaceX was off as much as 5% to about $147, below its first-day open of $150 and a day after one of its largest single-session value declines on record. Oil stayed soft — WTI settled down about 2.3% near $74.82 on Monday after Washington granted Iran a 60-day license to sell oil internationally — while the 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.5% after last week's more hawkish Fed under new Chair Kevin Warsh. Investors are looking ahead to Thursday's May PCE inflation report, the week's key macro test.
