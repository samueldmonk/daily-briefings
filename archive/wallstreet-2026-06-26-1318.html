The Lead
Markets open · as of ~1:00 PM ET

Indexes split as a defensive rotation cushions an AI-cost tech sell-off — as of ~1:00 PM ET

U.S. stocks are mixed in early-afternoon trade Friday after opening sharply lower and clawing much of the way back. As of roughly 1:00 PM ET the Dow Jones Industrial Average was edging higher while the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq Composite tread water — each having pared an early drop of more than 1% — and the small-cap Russell 2000 outperformed, up about 0.7%. The move is a rotation out of technology and into defensives — health care, industrials and materials. Even so, the major indexes are still tracking a losing week, with the S&P down more than 1% and the Nasdaq off about 4%, though the Dow is pacing for a roughly 0.6% weekly gain. The trigger: mounting worries over the cost of building out artificial-intelligence infrastructure, amplified by a report that OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its IPO to 2027 as it weighs a roughly $1 trillion valuation. In Tokyo, SoftBank tumbled about 12%, leading a rout in Asian tech that closed South Korea's Kospi down 5.8% after an 8% intraday drop tripped a circuit breaker.

The backdrop is a market on its back foot: on Thursday the Nasdaq Composite fell a fourth straight session — its first four-day losing streak since February — to 25,358.60, while the S&P 500 finished essentially flat at 7,357.49 and the Dow eked out a 0.14% gain to 51,920.62 after tagging a record intraday high. Friday's single biggest mover is ON Semiconductor, down about 23% after announcing a roughly $7 billion deal to acquire Synaptics, with Western Digital (≈ −11%) also sliding in a broad chip pullback that pulled Micron back about 2% after its Thursday earnings surge. Bucking the tape, Moderna jumped nearly 12% — the S&P's top performer — as investors digested its investor day. Megacaps stayed heavy, with Apple ≈ −6% (lineup-wide price hikes) and Microsoft ≈ −3.5% (an Xbox price increase) dragging Big Tech, while health care, industrials and materials led to the upside. Crude kept sliding — WTI near $69.40, down about 3.5% — while the 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.4%. Markets are parsing the University of Michigan's final June consumer-sentiment reading (consensus ~48.9, near historic lows) due today.
