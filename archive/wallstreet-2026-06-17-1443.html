The Lead

Hawkish hold: the Fed stands pat but signals a possible 2026 hike, and stocks pull back (as of ~2:45 p.m. ET)

The Federal Reserve left its benchmark rate unchanged at 3.50–3.75% in a unanimous vote at the close of Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting — exactly as markets had priced (CME FedWatch had the odds above 96%). But the message around the hold was notably hawkish: the updated "dot plot" showed nine of 18 officials now projecting a rate hike later in 2026, the committee eliminated the single rate cut that had appeared in March's projections, and it dropped its prior easing bias from the policy statement. With inflation running hot and the economy resilient, the Fed signaled rates are likely on hold — or higher — through year-end rather than coming down.

Stocks pulled back on the hawkish tilt. In the minutes after the 2 p.m. decision the S&P 500 slipped about 0.5%, the Nasdaq Composite about 0.7% and the Dow roughly 160 points (~0.3%), erasing a modestly higher midday tape — earlier in the session the indices had gyrated little-changed, with small caps the standout swing. The reaction underscored that, after four straight stronger-than-expected inflation months and the Iran-war energy shock, the burden of proof has shifted from "when do cuts start" to "could the next move be up."

The morning's data had already leaned hawkish: May retail sales rose 0.9% from April — well above the 0.5% expected and up 6.9% from a year earlier — with online sales up 1.5% and the core "control group" up 0.7%, a resilient-consumer read. The backdrop is a market that split sharply Tuesday, when the Dow set a fresh record close (51,999.67, +0.64%) as the only major index green while the S&P 500 (-0.08%), Nasdaq Composite (-1.15%) and Russell 2000 (-0.63%) slipped on a megacap-tech pullback — itself following Monday's surge (S&P +1.65%, Nasdaq +3.07%, Dow +0.92%) on President Trump's U.S.–Iran de-escalation framework. Reports say the two sides signed a memorandum of understanding, with a formal ceremony still reported for Friday in Switzerland though Iran has not publicly confirmed final terms. Crude has kept falling: West Texas Intermediate slid below $76 to about $75 a barrel, a fifth straight down session and its lowest since early March, now that tankers can transit the Strait again.
