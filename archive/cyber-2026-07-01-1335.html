Top Story

CISA flags a 10.0 SimpleHelp flaw as attackers weaponize remote-support servers

CISA has added CVE-2026-48558, a critical authentication-bypass vulnerability in the SimpleHelp remote-support platform, to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog. The flaw (CVSS v3.1 10.0) affects SimpleHelp 5.5.15 and earlier and 6.0 pre-release builds: when OIDC authentication is enabled, the server fails to verify the cryptographic signature of identity tokens, letting a remote, unauthenticated attacker forge a token and log in as a fully privileged "Technician" — and, in some configurations, self-register MFA to bypass it entirely.

Because SimpleHelp is widely deployed by IT departments, managed service providers and help desks, a compromised server hands attackers the same reach as a legitimate technician across every managed endpoint. BlackPoint's Adversary Pursuit Group has observed in-the-wild intrusions beginning with confirmed exploitation of this flaw, deploying two newly identified malware samples — the TaskWeaver loader and Djinn Stealer. The bug was discovered by Horizon3.ai's Zach Hanley (with help from generative AI) and is fixed in versions 5.5.16 and 6.0 RC2; exposed SimpleHelp servers have grown from roughly 3,400 in January 2025 to nearly 14,000, about 7.2% of them running the vulnerable OIDC configuration.

Patch Priority
Do this first · deadline tomorrow

Patch SimpleHelp to 5.5.16 / 6.0 RC2 now — CVE-2026-48558 (CVSS 10.0)

A max-severity flaw is being actively exploited, and CISA orders federal civilian agencies to remediate by July 2, 2026 (tomorrow). If you run SimpleHelp with OIDC enabled, upgrade immediately, review Horizon3.ai's published indicators of compromise, and hunt for rogue Technician accounts and the TaskWeaver/Djinn Stealer artifacts.

Threat Actor Spotlight

ShinyHunters — data-extortion crew tied to an Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day

The prolific ShinyHunters extortion group remains one of the week's most active threats. The crew is claiming theft of more than 9 million records from medical-device maker Medtronic in an April data-theft incident, and the company has begun notifying affected patients. Separately, attackers wielding an Oracle PeopleSoft zero-day have claimed high-profile victims including automaker Nissan and the U.S. National Association of Insurance Commissioners — a reminder that unpatched enterprise middleware keeps handing extortion crews the keys to sensitive data.
