The Lead

Dow closes at a record high as jobs miss; a second chip selloff drags the Nasdaq lower

Wall Street split on Thursday. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at a record high, jumping 594.83 points (1.14%) to 52,900.07 — its first all-time closing high of the summer — while a second straight session of semiconductor selling dragged the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite down 0.80% (−207.36) to 25,832.67. The S&P 500 finished essentially flat at 7,483.24, as strength in financials and other blue-chip sectors offset the slide in technology. The divide told the story: information technology — semiconductors in particular — lagged for a second straight session, while non-tech blue chips powered the Dow to its record close.

The catalyst was a cooler-than-expected June jobs report that Wall Street read as easing pressure for another Fed rate hike. U.S. employers added just 57,000 positions in June — well short of the roughly 115,000 economists expected — and April and May were revised down by a combined 74,000. The unemployment rate came in at 4.2% (below the 4.3% consensus) even as labor-force participation slipped to 61.5% — its lowest since March 2021. The read mattered because the Fed — which held its benchmark at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 (Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting) with a "dot plot" that leaned toward a rate hike — now faces a cooling labor market, and the soft print eased fears of that hike.

The drag again came from the AI-hardware trade: chip and semiconductor names sold off for a second straight session. The unwind was sparked midweek, when glass-and-fiber supplier Corning — a key AI–data-center play — plunged about 13% on Wednesday; AI-infrastructure names stayed under pressure Thursday. Money kept rotating toward blue chips and financials, powering the Dow to its record close, even as the tech-led Nasdaq finished lower on the day. U.S. markets are closed Friday, July 3 for the Independence Day holiday; trading resumes Monday, July 6.
