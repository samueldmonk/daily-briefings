The Lead
Markets closed for the weekend · last session Friday, June 26 · after the bell

Stocks finished little changed Friday after clawing back a chip-led drop; the S&P and Nasdaq booked a losing week

With U.S. markets shut for the weekend, Friday's close stands as the latest read. Stocks ended the session close to the flatline after opening sharply lower and recovering nearly all of an early, chip-led decline of more than 1%. The S&P 500 slipped 0.05% to 7,354.02 and the Dow Jones Industrial Average eased 44.51 points (0.09%) to 51,876.11, while the Nasdaq Composite fell 0.24% to 25,297.62 — its fifth straight losing session — weighed by semiconductors; the small-cap Russell 2000 outperformed, up about 0.7%. The bounce-back masked a rough five days: the S&P 500 slid nearly 2% on the week and the Nasdaq dropped about 4.6%, though the Dow held a roughly 0.6% weekly gain. The trigger was the one that rattled the tape all week: mounting worry over the cost of building out artificial-intelligence infrastructure, amplified by a New York Times report that OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its IPO to next year — reportedly wary of the weak post-debut showing of newly public SpaceX (down about 1.6%) and broad volatility in AI-related shares.

Friday's single biggest decliner was ON Semiconductor, which tumbled about 22% after agreeing to acquire Synaptics (roughly flat) in an all-stock deal valued at about $7 billion — its largest-ever acquisition — leading a broad chip pullback that also pressured Western Digital (≈ −11%), Micron, Nvidia and AMD as investors questioned the pace of AI-infrastructure spending. To the upside, Moderna jumped to the top of the S&P 500 (up roughly 12%) after an FDA advisory committee voted 9–0 that its mRNA flu vaccine (mRNA-1010) has a favorable benefit-risk profile — a strong signal ahead of an August 5 decision date — while its annual Science Day detailed a broadening pipeline reaching well beyond vaccines into oncology and autoimmune disease (including an in vivo CAR-T); FactSet and ServiceNow also rallied. The rate backdrop turned more hawkish: May inflation came in at 4.2% year-over-year — the highest in three years — and traders now price roughly a 38% chance of a quarter-point Fed rate hike at the September FOMC (versus about 56% for no change), with 2026 rate-cut bets all but gone. The 10-year Treasury yield held below 4.5% and WTI crude slipped below $70 a barrel (intraday near $69).
