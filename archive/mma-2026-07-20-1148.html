Top Story

Du Plessis wants the belt back — but tells Strickland to fight Imavov first

Dricus du Plessis battered former welterweight champion Kamaru Usman over five rounds for a unanimous-decision win (50–45, 49–46, 49–46) in the main event of UFC Fight Night 281 at Paycom Center in Oklahoma City on Saturday, July 18 — his first bout since losing the middleweight title to Khamzat Chimaev last August, and a result that moves him to 24–3.

In the cage he said, “I said a win wouldn't give me a title shot, but damn, a performance like that would get me a title shot.” At the post-fight press conference he doubled down, saying he “absolutely” deserves the next crack at champion Sean Strickland. Then he complicated his own case: “Fight Imavov and get a title defense, he earned that and after that I'll come and take my belt back.” The tension between those two positions is the story.

Du Plessis holds two wins over Strickland, including one title defense, which is the strongest part of his argument. Nassourdine Imavov, riding a five-fight run that includes a TKO of Israel Adesanya and a unanimous decision over Caio Borralho, has publicly shut down the idea of being leapfrogged, per Bloody Elbow's July 19 report. Khamzat Chimaev is still pushing for an immediate rematch after his split-decision loss to Strickland at UFC 328 in May; du Plessis has called that booking “senseless.” Dana White has not named a challenger.

On the fight itself: du Plessis wobbled Usman repeatedly across the first two rounds with clubbing strikes and knees up the middle, and improved takedown defense shut down Usman's wrestling. Usman found some confidence in the third, and du Plessis showed fatigue late after an eleven-month layoff.

Rankings & Business

  • The rankings themselves changed this summer, which is why this briefing has been cautious with numbers. On June 20, 2026 the UFC began replacing its traditional media-panel rankings with the Meta UFC Rankings, an Elo-style mathematical model built in partnership with Meta. It weights opponent quality and activity, decays a fighter's rating if they do not compete within 18 months, and progressively discounts wins older than five years. Pound-for-pound rankings are removed and the media panel continues only through a transition period.
  • The model is not AI at runtime. A UFC representative: “We didn't build an A.I. to rank fighters. A.I. was used in creating the system, but on a week to week basis, when you see the rankings come out, it's not because A.I. said so. A mathematical model is doing the work on the back end, computing things and spitting out numbers.”
  • Rankings movement: for the July 25 main event, the UFC's own event page lists Ankalaev at No. 1 and Guskov at No. 9. LowKick's preview differs, calling Ankalaev a former champion and current No. 2 contender and Guskov No. 10. Both are reported rather than reconciled. Sources this run give Ankalaev's record as 21–2–1 and Guskov's as 18–3–1, while a prior preview listed Ankalaev at 20–2 — the discrepancy is flagged, not smoothed over.
  • Middleweight positioning is likewise unsettled in coverage: du Plessis is reported at No. 2 with Usman at No. 9 and Imavov at No. 3, and Chimaev at No. 1, but this run's sources have conflicted on the exact figures across editions, so they are carried as reported rather than asserted.
  • Bonus structure: since January 2026 Performance of the Night and Fight of the Night awards have paid $100,000, up from $50,000.
  • Business: no gate, viewership or TKO Group figures were sourced this run, so none are published. The last gate figure this briefing verified remains UFC 329's $26,430,566.
