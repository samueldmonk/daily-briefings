The Lead
Markets closed · Friday, June 26 · after the bell

Stocks finish little changed after clawing back a chip-led drop; the S&P and Nasdaq book a losing week

U.S. stocks ended Friday close to the flatline after opening sharply lower and recovering nearly all of an early, chip-led decline of more than 1%. The S&P 500 and the Dow Jones Industrial Average finished roughly flat while the Nasdaq Composite was the marginal laggard, weighed by semiconductors; the small-cap Russell 2000 outperformed, up about 0.7%. The bounce-back masked a rough five days: the S&P 500 fell more than 1% on the week and the Nasdaq dropped about 4% — its worst week in some time — though the Dow held a roughly 0.6% weekly gain. The trigger was the same one that has rattled the tape all week: mounting worry over the cost of building out artificial-intelligence infrastructure, amplified by a New York Times report that OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its IPO to next year — reportedly wary of the weak post-debut showing of newly public SpaceX (down about 1.6%) and broad volatility in AI-related shares.

Friday's single biggest decliner was ON Semiconductor, which tumbled about 19% after agreeing to acquire Synaptics (up ~4.7%) in an all-stock deal valued at roughly $7 billion — its largest-ever acquisition — leading a broad chip pullback that dragged the Philadelphia Semiconductor index down 4.7%, with Micron off about 6.2% (paring Thursday's blockbuster post-earnings surge) and Nvidia and AMD lower. Apple slipped about 0.1% after a 6.1% slump Thursday — its biggest one-day drop in over a year. To the upside, Moderna jumped about 12% — the S&P 500's top performer — on a Science Day that showcased an expanded mRNA pipeline across oncology, autoimmune and rare disease, joined by biotech strength in SELLAS (≈ +13%) and Acadia (≈ +12%, on EU approval of its Rett-syndrome therapy). The rate backdrop turned more hawkish: Thursday's data showed May inflation topped 4% for the first time in three years, and traders now price roughly a 68% chance of a quarter-point Fed rate hike by September — up from about 29% a week ago. The 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.4% and WTI crude hovered around $70 a barrel.
