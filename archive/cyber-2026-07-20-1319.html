Top Story

SonicWall SMA zero-days were exploited for three weeks before disclosure

Two vulnerabilities in SonicWall's SMA1000 series secure-access gateways are under active exploitation, and the pairing is what makes them dangerous. CVE-2026-15409 is a server-side request forgery flaw in the SMA 1000 Work Place interface, rated CVSS 10.0, that lets a remote and unauthenticated attacker force the appliance to make network requests to locations of the attacker's choosing. CVE-2026-15410 is a code-injection flaw in the Appliance Management Console — the administrative interface used to configure the appliance, manage users, set access policies and monitor sessions — rated CVSS 7.2 and nominally requiring authentication. Chained, they reach arbitrary command execution and full device takeover.

The material development this run comes from Volexity, which attributes pre-disclosure exploitation to a previously undocumented threat actor it is tracking as UTA0533. Per researchers Sean Koessel and Steven Adair, the activity dates back to June 22, 2026 — roughly three weeks before SonicWall published its advisory on July 14. The discovery came out of an incident-response investigation earlier this month; the affected organization has not been named. Volexity describes the actor as using multiple zero-day exploits together with malware built specifically for SonicWall SMA VPN appliances.

That timeline changes the remediation question. Patching closes the door going forward, but it does nothing about a foothold established in late June. Rapid7's MDR team, which independently discovered the exploitation and tied related activity to an actor connected to the Inc ransomware-as-a-service group, documented what attackers take once inside: high-value credentials, active session databases and TOTP multi-factor authentication seed configurations. All three survive a hotfix. Affected models named in this run's coverage include the SMA6210, SMA7210 and SMA8200v. If you ran an internet-facing SMA1000 at any point since late June, credential rotation and MFA re-enrolment are part of the remediation, not an optional extra — and the hunt window starts in June, not July.

Patch Priority

Overdue — act now

SonicWall SMA1000: CVE-2026-15409 & CVE-2026-15410 — CISA deadline was July 17

CISA added both CVEs to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on July 14, 2026 with a federal remediation deadline of Friday, July 17, 2026 — a three-day window, assigned per-CVE under the risk-based BOD 26-04 rather than the older flat three-week rule. That deadline is now —. SonicWall's hotfix is available. This is the single most urgent item on the board: maximum severity, unauthenticated, confirmed ransomware use — and now confirmed to have been live as a zero-day since June 22.

Secondary but also past due: CVE-2026-58644, the CVSS 9.8 Microsoft SharePoint Server deserialization flaw exploited as a zero-day before patches existed, was added to KEV on July 16 with a July 19 deadline — now —.

Breaches & Incidents

NewUnauth RCE

wp2shell: anonymous code execution in WordPress core

An anonymous HTTP request can run code on a WordPress site — and because the bug is in core, a bare install with zero plugins is exploitable. wp2shell is two bugs chained: CVE-2026-63030, a REST API batch-route confusion, and CVE-2026-60137, a SQL injection in core. Every 6.9 and 7.0 site was in range until Friday, when WordPress shipped 6.9.5 and 7.0.2 and enabled forced updates through its auto-update system. Since then the full mechanism has been published and a working proof-of-concept is public on GitHub, so the exploitation window is wide open for anyone who has auto-updates disabled. Adam Kues at Assetnote, Searchlight Cyber's attack-surface arm, found the batch-route bug.

NewCloud credentials

NadMesh botnet hunts exposed AI services

A Go botnet that surfaced in early July and goes looking specifically for self-hosted AI infrastructure. A Shodan harvester keeps its scan queue stocked with ComfyUI, Ollama, n8n, Open WebUI, Langflow and Gradio — the model runners and workflow builders teams stand up quickly and firewall late. The operator's own dashboard claims 3,811 unique AWS keys; the intel feed behind it shows 47 credential hauls and 41 model inventories in its last 100 records. QiAnXin's XLab published the report and screenshotted the panel. Treat the numbers with care: they are the operator's own, captured July 10, and they do not agree with each other — a counter reading 17,700 total deploys sits above a funnel claiming 95,700 in 24 hours.

NewSupply chain

ViteVenom: seven malicious npm packages, blockchain C2

Checkmarx found a cluster of seven malicious npm packages typosquatting the Vite frontend tooling ecosystem. The campaign is an expansion of ChainVeil, which researchers describe as using an unprecedented four-tier blockchain-based command-and-control infrastructure spanning Tron, Aptos and Binance Smart Chain to deliver a RAT capable of reverse shell, credential harvesting, file exfiltration and persistent backdoor injection. The blockchain layer is the point: as Checkmarx's Pavan Gudimalla puts it, the tactic makes disabling or destroying the C2 infrastructure extremely difficult. Attributed to an actor tracked as SuccessKey, with linked crypto wallets active as far back as February 27, 2026.

NewState-sponsored

Sandworm sub-cluster runs ClickFix on Ukrainian targets

CERT-UA attributes a ClickFix campaign against Ukrainian targets to UAC-0145, a sub-cluster within Sandworm — the GRU-affiliated unit inside Russia's foreign military intelligence service. The technique is social engineering rather than exploitation: fake CAPTCHA checks on compromised websites instruct the visitor to paste and run a PowerShell command in their own terminal. One observed variant downloads a VBS file into the Startup autorun directory; CERT-UA names that program GHETTOVIBE. The attacks also deploy SCOUTCURL, a PowerShell reconnaissance script. No vulnerability is involved, which is precisely why patching does not help here.

NewDenial of service

OpenSSL HollowByte: 11 bytes, 131 KB gone

Eleven bytes will make an unpatched OpenSSL server set aside up to 131 KB of memory for a message that never arrives — and on the glibc systems Okta tested, that memory is gone until the process restarts. The flaw is that OpenSSL trusted the attacker's declared length: every TLS handshake message carries a 4-byte header, three bytes of which state the body length, and older versions grew the receive buffer to that size before a single byte of the body landed. The awkward part for defenders is the disclosure: OpenSSL shipped the fix in June with no CVE, no advisory and no changelog entry pointing at it. Fixed releases are 4.0.1, 3.6.3, 3.5.7, 3.4.6 and 3.0.21, all dated June 9. Nothing in a normal patch pipeline will flag it — there is no identifier for a scanner to match. Okta's Red Team reported and named it.

Healthcare

Craneware: hospital billing vendor confirms data theft

Edinburgh-based Craneware, whose accounting and billing software is used by thousands of U.S. hospitals, clinics and pharmacies — more than 2,000 U.S. hospitals by one account — confirmed data was stolen in a cyberattack. The company did not specify what was taken, saying only that a “percentage” of employee data, customer data and partner records was exfiltrated, along with a significant volume of file names. Its current assessment is that a large element of the data involved is non-sensitive or already-public regulatory data. Craneware says the intruders appear to have been expelled, the incident is contained, customer services were not disrupted, and it has notified the UK ICO and the FBI. The investigation is ongoing, which is the caveat worth holding: “a percentage” is not a scope.

Supply chain

SleeperGem backdoors three RubyGems packages

Between July 18 and 19, malicious versions of three RubyGems packages were published to RubyGems.org in a coordinated campaign researchers named SleeperGem. The compromised gems are git_credential_manager (impersonating Microsoft's Git Credential Manager), Dendreo, and fastlane-plugin-run_tests_firebase_testlab. Each release is a loader that pulls a second stage from an attacker-controlled Forgejo host, then drops a native daemon and installs persistence. The targeting is the notable part: the malware checks roughly thirty CI environment variables — GITHUB_ACTIONS, GITLAB_CI, CIRCLECI among them — and exits if it finds any. The operators want developer laptops, not ephemeral CI runners. Anyone who installed these gems should treat the machine and every secret reachable from it as compromised.

Supply chain

DigiCert certificate theft traced to CylindricalCanine

Researchers analysed CylindricalCanine, a subgroup of the Chinese cybercrime collective GoldenEyeDog (also tracked as APT-Q-27, Dragon Breath and Miuuti Group), and linked it to DigiCert's April 2026 compromise. Per Expel's Aaron Walton, the actor used its malware to reach a support member's device at DigiCert and leveraged that access to steal code-signing certificates intended for DigiCert customers — leading to 60 revocations, including at least 27 associated with malware. Central to the operation is a modified Gh0st RAT (aka Farfli). Stolen signing certificates let malicious binaries inherit the trust of a legitimate publisher, which is why the revocation count matters more than the intrusion itself.

Ransomware

Fairlife production halted

Coca-Cola's U.S. dairy subsidiary Fairlife confirmed a ransomware attack that temporarily halted production across the United States. Attackers accessed systems supporting manufacturing operations, prompting the company to activate incident response and business continuity procedures. Coca-Cola filed an SEC Form 8-K on 2026-07-16.

Ransomware

Spirals: under 24 hours from access to encryption

A Rust-based ransomware family used against a South Asian IT services company. The operators moved from initial access to full network encryption in less than 24 hours, using an IIS web shell, WMI and PsExec to spread, disabling security services and disrupting backups before encrypting. The dwell time is the story: a daily backup cadence would not have survived it.

Third party

Ernst & Young support platform breach

An unauthorized third party breached an IT-staff support-ticket platform. Anomalous activity was identified April 23, 2026, with an access window of March 28 – April 12, 2026. Tickets held client tax data plus personal information tied to investment holdings with EY's institutional clients, potentially including SSNs and financial account codes. California AG notification came July 15; individual notifications began July 13, with Experian IdentityWorks offered. No current evidence of misuse.
