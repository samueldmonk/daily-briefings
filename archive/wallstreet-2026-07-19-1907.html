The Lead

Chips close the week in a bear market — and the tape sits there until Monday

U.S. equity markets are shut for the weekend. There is no live session to report; the most recent one was Friday, July 17, and it ended badly. The S&P 500 fell 1.01% to 7,457.69, the Nasdaq Composite dropped 1.40% to 25,520.24 and the Dow slipped 406.55 points, or 0.77%, to 52,146.42.

The damage was concentrated in semiconductors. A key industry gauge — the PHLX Semiconductor Index — finished 20% below its record close, the technical definition of a bear market, capping the cohort's worst week since April 2025. The Motley Fool's recap notes the same index remains up roughly 63% year to date, which is the other half of the story: this is a violent unwind of an enormous run rather than a collapse from a standing start.

Two catalysts were named repeatedly. A surprise release from Chinese AI start-up Moonshot jolted the sector on fears it undercuts U.S. vendors' pricing power, and a re-escalation of geopolitical tension drove a broad risk-off tone while pushing crude sharply higher.

Breadth was narrow. Energy was the only S&P 500 sector to finish higher, with communication stocks and consumer cyclicals hit hardest; CNBC's sector read put energy at +1.82%, information technology at −2.65% and communication services at −1.45%. For the week the S&P 500 lost about 1.6% — its first losing week in the last three, and only its third since March — while the Nasdaq fell 2.90% and the Dow gave up roughly 0.9%.

Movers & Drivers

+9%Earnings

Travelers jumps on a blowout second quarter

The Travelers Companies jumped about 9% on a large Q2 earnings beat, per the Motley Fool and TheStreet recaps; CNBC's midday movers list had it up "more than 8%", with EPS of $10.04 against a $5.41 consensus and roughly $2.2 billion of net income versus about $1.5 billion a year earlier. Insurance peers Progressive and Allstate also gained — one of the few pockets of green on the day.

LowerSemis

Nvidia slides as the AI rotation bites

Nvidia fell as investors rotated out of the AI bellwether, with the semiconductor sell-off also dragging on Advanced Micro Devices and Intel. CNBC's midday movers put Nvidia down 3.5% at a market value near $4.86 trillion. The move came alongside the PHLX Semiconductor Index's slide into bear-market territory.

Mega-capNew

Apple briefly took the crown — then gave it back

Apple passed Nvidia intraday on Friday to become, momentarily, the world's most valuable company, with both around $4.9 trillion. The positions later reversed and Nvidia closed slightly above Apple, so this was a brief intraday crossing rather than a change of rank at the bell. Apple is up roughly 23% year to date; Nvidia had held the title since June 2025. Exact market caps differ across outlets, so only the approximate figure is given here.

LowerAI

Alphabet falls on a reported Gemini delay

Alphabet closed at $346.12, down about 2.2%, following reports that the launch of Gemini 3.5 Pro had been delayed. Netflix also plunged during the session on its earnings response — no verified closing percentage was available for it this run, so it is left qualitative rather than quantified.

On the Radar

  • FOMC, July 28–29. The decision lands at 2:00 p.m. ET on the second day with the Chair's press conference at 2:30; there is no Summary of Economic Projections at this meeting. The CME FedWatch tool prices roughly an 89% probability of a hold, which would leave the target range at 3.50%–3.75%.

  • Q2 earnings. FactSet's forecast for S&P 500 second-quarter earnings growth is +23.1% year over year. Insurance has set a high bar early; the semiconductor complex reports into a tape that has already de-rated it.
  • The chip narrative is the swing factor. Whether Moonshot's model is a genuine pricing threat or a sentiment shock is the question that decides if Friday's bear-market print in the PHLX Semiconductor Index extends or reverses.
