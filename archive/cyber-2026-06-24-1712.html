Top Story

Microsoft and Europol disrupt the Amadey and StealC infostealer networks

In a coordinated action announced June 24, Microsoft's Digital Crimes Unit, working with Europol, Germany's Federal Criminal Police Office (BKA) and industry partners including Bitsight, ESET, IBM X-Force, Lumen and Proofpoint, disrupted the shared infrastructure behind two of the most prolific commodity infostealers, Amadey and StealC. Amadey typically establishes the initial foothold on a victim's machine while StealC harvests passwords and other sensitive data; together they were linked to more than 140,000 infected computers globally in just the first two weeks of May 2026. The latest chapter of Operation Endgame, the action took down command-and-control servers and seized domains through a mix of court orders and provider notifications.

By the numbers, the operation dismantled 326 servers and 142 domains, disrupted more than 200 malicious Amadey and StealC command-and-control servers, and severed criminal control of over 18,000 victim computers. Investigators identified and froze criminal cryptocurrency worth over €41 million (about $46.5 million) across a wider sweep that also hit SocGholish, and recovered roughly 27 million stolen login credentials taken from more than 385,000 compromised systems. Notably, Microsoft paired the technical takedown with a racketeering lawsuit — what reporters describe as the first U.S. court action to target two distinct cybercrime tools at once. Defenders should still treat any credentials touched by these stealers as compromised: rotate passwords, revoke active sessions and enforce MFA.

Patch Priority

Patch Ubiquiti UniFi OS now — CVE-2026-34910 (CVSS 10.0) is exploited; federal deadline June 26

The single most urgent action for defenders today is the max-severity (CVSS 10.0) Ubiquiti UniFi OS set. CVE-2026-34910 is an improper-input-validation flaw that lets a network-adjacent attacker run command injection; it ships alongside an improper-access-control bug (CVE-2026-34908) and a path-traversal flaw (CVE-2026-34909) that researchers at Bishop Fox showed can be chained to full remote code execution with elevated privileges. CISA added all three to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on June 23 based on evidence of active exploitation; under BOD 26-04, federal agencies must apply Ubiquiti's fixes (update UniFi OS to 5.0.8 or later) or discontinue the affected products by June 26, 2026 (two days out). Apply the updates immediately, especially where UniFi management interfaces are reachable from untrusted networks. Note: the actively-exploited Splunk Enterprise pre-auth RCE (CVE-2026-20253) is already past its June 21 deadline — patch it (10.4.0 / 10.2.4 / 10.0.7) if you have not.

Threat Actor Spotlight

FortiBleed's operator — a Russian-speaking initial access broker

The actor behind the FortiBleed campaign is assessed to be a Russian-speaking initial access broker running a large-scale, automated operation against internet-facing Fortinet FortiGate firewalls and SSL-VPN gateways — active since around February 2026 and reaching 194 countries. Rather than exploiting a single new vulnerability, the operator runs a credential pipeline: credential stuffing and password spraying from earlier breach dumps and infostealer logs, configuration harvesting, offline hash-cracking, and post-authentication capture of further credentials. An attacker-controlled database holds valid administrator credentials for tens of thousands of devices — Recorded Future counts 73,932 FortiGate firewalls, SecurityWeek/SOCRadar put the tally near 86,644 — organized by country, sector and revenue. Independent researcher Bob Diachenko found the exposed server on June 13; the campaign was disclosed June 16. Fortinet stresses this is credential reuse and brute-forcing, not a new CVE (there is nothing to patch); CISA's June 18 advisory urges credential rotation, MFA and hardened management interfaces.

Breaches & Incidents

Aflac discloses major data theft

U.S. insurance giant Aflac disclosed that data on nearly 22.7 million customers, beneficiaries, employees and agents was stolen following a cyberattack in June, making it one of the larger breaches of the year by people affected. Customers should watch for follow-on phishing and identity-fraud attempts and consider credit monitoring; the scope and notification process are still being detailed.

LastPass & others via the Klue supply chain

LastPass confirmed that customer CRM data in its Salesforce instance was exposed through a breach of third-party vendor Klue, claimed by a new extortion group calling itself "Icarus." The same OAuth-token compromise reached BeyondTrust, Recorded Future, Tanium, Jamf, Sprout Social, Gong and Insurity. Exposed data was limited to names, contact details and support/sales records; LastPass says password vaults and master passwords were not affected, and Salesforce disabled the Klue integration.

London Hydro discloses customer data breach

Canadian electricity provider London Hydro said on June 20 that attackers broke into its systems, potentially exposing customers' names, addresses, emails and phone numbers along with account, billing and meter details. The utility is investigating and notifying affected customers — the latest reminder that critical-infrastructure operators remain prime targets.

ShinyHunters' Canvas theft — largest education breach on record

The extortion crew ShinyHunters remains tied to the year's largest breach by record count — the Instructure / Canvas incident, with roughly 275 million records across about 8,809 school districts, universities and online-education platforms. The group runs pure data-theft-and-extortion and reached a May 11 agreement with Instructure; the FBI has warned students and staff to expect follow-on extortion and phishing. Also active this week: malicious npm packages from a user "abdrizak" dropping a Windows RAT.

"Cordyceps" CI/CD flaw & "The Gentlemen" EDR-killers

Researchers flagged a CI/CD workflow weakness — codenamed Cordyceps — that lets any unauthenticated user forge approvals, push code or steal credentials (GitHub is hardening actions/checkout against "pwn request" patterns). Separately, the "The Gentlemen" ransomware-as-a-service operation is actively developing EDR killers handed to affiliates to disable defenses before the encryptor deploys.
