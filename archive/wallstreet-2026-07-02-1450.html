The Lead

Stocks split after a soft jobs report: the Dow leads while a second day of chip selling drags the Nasdaq (as of ~2:30 PM ET)

A cooler-than-expected June employment report pulled Wall Street in two directions on Thursday. As of about 2:30 PM ET, the Dow Jones Industrial Average held a gain of roughly 0.5% and led the majors, while the S&P 500 sat essentially flat (about −0.01%) and the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite fell roughly 1.3% — its second straight day of semiconductor-driven declines as investors kept rotating out of the red-hot AI-hardware trade. Beneath the flat headline, breadth was actually positive: about 354 of the S&P 500's 500 members were higher, led by health care, consumer staples and utilities, even as heavyweight chip names weighed on the cap-weighted index. Traders squared up ahead of the long holiday weekend, with U.S. markets shut Friday, July 3.

The catalyst was the jobs data: U.S. employers added just 57,000 positions in June, well short of the roughly 113,000 economists expected, and April and May were revised down by a combined 74,000. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% (from 4.3%), and average hourly earnings rose 0.3% on the month (3.5% year over year). The soft print revived hopes the Fed will stay on hold: short-term yields eased and traders trimmed rate-hike bets under Chair Kevin Warsh, whose committee is holding the funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% and who said this week that "inflation risks have come down."

Movers & Drivers

Rivian ▲ ~13%

The session's standout gainer: Rivian jumped nearly 13% to about $19.38 after the EV maker raised its 2026 delivery guidance to 65,000–70,000 vehicles (from 62,000–67,000), citing stronger-than-expected second-quarter demand. It is today's Chart of the Day.

Semiconductors ▼ 2nd day

The dominant drag: chip and AI-hardware names extended their sell-off for a second straight session, pulling the Nasdaq lower as the crowded semiconductor trade kept unwinding. Citi added to the caution, issuing a warning on semiconductor and hyperscaler stocks.

Bitcoin ▲ ~5%

The world's largest cryptocurrency surged about 5% to roughly $61,275 as inflation fears eased, after Fed Chair Kevin Warsh said energy prices — and "inflation risks" — "have come down" since the U.S.–Iran de-escalation.
