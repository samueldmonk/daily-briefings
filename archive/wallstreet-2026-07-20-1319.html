The Lead

Chips lift Wall Street — but only in America

Wall Street is holding steadier as chip companies and other winners of the AI boom swing back upward, the Associated Press reported in a breaking update timestamped 9:34 a.m. ET. AP had the S&P 500 up 0.6%, the Dow Jones Industrial Average up 190 points and the Nasdaq Composite up 0.8%, with Nvidia up 1.6% after serving as the heaviest weight on the S&P 500 on Friday. The index is coming off its first losing week in the last three, and only its third since the end of March.

That AP read is newly fetched this edition and reconciles with the Reuters anchor earlier editions carried: at 9:50 a.m. ET Reuters put the Dow up 78.39 points (+0.15%) at 52,224.81, the S&P 500 up 44.81 points (+0.60%) at 7,502.50, and the Nasdaq Composite up 234.45 points (+0.92%) at 25,754.69. Both anchors are morning marks. No later timestamped cash reading could be verified this run — the live widgets above carry the current tape; the editorial does not pretend to.

The more interesting fact is what is not rebounding. The same session that has U.S. chipmakers recovering saw South Korea's Kospi fall 4.5% to 6,516.27, with Samsung Electronics down 4.3% and memory maker SK Hynix down 4.2%. Taiwan's Taiex slipped 0.5% even as TSMC rose 1.3% in Taipei — a partial recovery from Friday's 7.3% drop, which followed its announcement of an additional $100 billion of U.S. chipmaking capacity. Hong Kong's Hang Seng rose 2.4% to 25,143.05 and the Shanghai Composite added 0.9% to 3,796.28. Japan was closed for a holiday.

AP attributes part of the Asian weakness to the rollout of another powerful Chinese AI model — Kimi K3, an open-source release from Beijing-based Moonshot AI — and likens the impact to China's DeepSeek moment in early 2025: a signal that lower-cost, capable Chinese models are increasingly challenging rivals like Anthropic's Claude and OpenAI's GPT. Oil, meanwhile, flipped from gains to losses even as the U.S. announced attacks on Iran for a ninth straight night.

A note on figures not published here. The futures-as-cash trap fired again and was re-proven directly this edition: a search synthesis reported "the Dow fell 0.2%, the S&P added 0.3%, the Nasdaq gained 0.6%" as a midday cash read, and a direct fetch of the underlying Yahoo Finance article showed it to be a 7:24 a.m. ET pre-open piece about index futures (page header: "U.S. markets open in 1h 56m"), in which Dow futures were up 0.2%. Separately, syntheses again offered "S&P 500 +0.63%, Dow +0.23%, Nasdaq +1.02%" — once with an invented "as of 1 PM ET" attached. Those are figures earlier editions carried this morning, re-served without a real timestamp. Both rejected; neither appears above.

Movers & Drivers

New~+15% off-hours

AMC Entertainment — record quarter, and an 85-hour weekend

AMC soared close to 15% in off-hours trading after the movie theater chain posted record second-quarter sales and profit, per AP, and rose again once the regular session opened. The quarter's figures do not even include this past weekend, when the 106-year-old company said 4.3 million people watched The Odyssey in its theaters — six of its locations ran the Christopher Nolan-directed historical epic non-stop for 85 hours straight to meet demand. A separate search-sourced reading put AMC up 9% in early trading; that figure carries no verified timestamp, so the off-hours move is the one published here.

New+1.6%

Nvidia — the heaviest weight, reversed

Nvidia rose 1.6% in early Monday trading after being the single heaviest drag on the S&P 500 on Friday, per AP's 9:34 a.m. ET update. It is the clearest single-name expression of the day's rotation back into the AI complex after a week in which the sector's momentum broke. No later intraday figure was verified this run.

NewKospi −4.5%

Asian memory complex — the other side of the trade

The rebound did not travel. South Korea's Kospi sank 4.5% to 6,516.27, an index that has benefited substantially from the global AI frenzy, with its two most valuable stocks both lower: Samsung Electronics −4.3% and memory chipmaker SK Hynix −4.2%. AP ties the move in part to Moonshot AI's Kimi K3 open-source release. For anyone reading the U.S. chip bounce as a sector all-clear, this is the counter-evidence in the same session.

New+1.3% Taipei

TSMC — partial recovery after a $100bn Friday

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. climbed 1.3% in Taipei, clawing back part of Friday's 7.3% drop. The Friday fall followed TSMC's announcement that it plans to spend an additional $100 billion expanding chipmaking capacity in the United States — a commitment the market initially read as margin pressure rather than demand confirmation. The wider Taiex still finished 0.5% lower.

Disputed

Domino's Pizza — revenue beat, direction contested

The revenue beat is not in dispute; the share move is. AP has Domino's jumping more than 7% premarket after beating second-quarter revenue projections and announcing another round of stock buybacks — while falling short of Wall Street's profit targets, with net income up 3.6% year on year. Reuters had it +3.6% at 9:50 a.m. ET; Investing.com's later strip showed +1.96%. Against all three, a Yahoo Finance trending-tickers capture showed DPZ at 322.18, −7.49, −2.27%. That Yahoo page rendered with a stale pre-open header, so its capture time cannot be verified. The four readings are stated, not reconciled.

~+5%

AMD — Microsoft locks in Helios

AMD jumped roughly 5% on a wide-scale Microsoft Azure deployment of its Helios rack-scale AI system, reported independently by Yahoo Finance, Investing.com, Invezz, Stocktwits and Blockonomi — the best-corroborated single-name catalyst of the session before AMC's results landed. AMD's own Advancing AI 2026 event follows July 22–23.

+2.2% Fri

Alphabet — custom silicon, and Wednesday's number

Alphabet added 3.4% on a report that the Google parent is developing new chips to run its own AI models, and closed Friday up 2.2% — one of only two S&P 500 names Zacks flagged as advancing in a session where ten of eleven sectors fell. Earnings land Wednesday, July 22, and the capital-expenditure line is what the AI trade is actually waiting on.

Memory leads

Micron and SanDisk — strip readings only

Micron and SanDisk were up 5.1% and 5.4% respectively in Reuters' account of the morning's chip leadership, and Investing.com's live strip showed SNDK +6.92% and MU +5.67%. These are single-source or single-widget readings rather than multi-source confirmations, and the Asian memory names moved the opposite way in the same session — both facts are on this page.

+1.62% pre

SpaceX — Starship 13 slips to Thursday

SpaceX (SPCX) rose 1.62% to $125.99 in premarket trading, per TheStreet. The company rescheduled the 13th test flight of Starship for Thursday, July 23 after a Raptor 3 engine ignition failure forced an automatic abort of the earlier attempt. Its June 12 IPO raised $75 billion, the largest in history, and the stock hit an all-time intraday high of $225.64 three trading days later. Only the premarket move is sourced; no intraday SPCX figure is published.

On the Radar

  • Megacap earnings week. Alphabet reports Wednesday, July 22, with Tesla, Intel and IBM also due. Capital.com's Daniela Hathorn frames the week plainly: after last week's sharp rotation out of AI-related stocks, "investors will be looking for evidence that earnings can continue to justify elevated valuations."
  • Kimi K3 and the second DeepSeek moment. Beijing-based Moonshot AI released a new open-source model that AP says rattled markets much as DeepSeek did in early 2025 — read as another sign that lower-cost Chinese models are challenging Anthropic's Claude and OpenAI's GPT. It is the cleanest single explanation on offer for why Asian AI-levered indexes fell while U.S. chips rose.
  • AMD's Advancing AI 2026 event, July 22–23. CNBC reports Meta, OpenAI and Oracle have already committed as early Helios platform adopters, and Jefferies analysts flagged Anthropic as a likely next AMD customer announcement.
  • FOMC meets July 28–29. CME's FedWatch tool puts the odds of a quarter-point hike at this month's meeting at about 15%. Capital Economics' Jonas Goltermann warns that "the return to war in the Strait of Hormuz may start to weigh more heavily on financial markets before too long, especially if even strong tech earnings reports continue to be met with skepticism."
  • U.S.–Iran escalation, ninth straight night. The U.S. announced further attacks early Monday and reported the death of a third American service member, killed in northern Iraq during the controlled detonation of a downed Iranian drone. Iran has been responding by striking U.S. allies across the region. Separately, Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei said Tehran has received proposals from mediators about resuming negotiations and would pursue them if they align with Iran's national interests, while continuing to defend itself "resolutely."
  • Friday's macro print, for the record. June housing starts rose to 1,427,000 (May revised up to 1,199,000); building permits fell to 1,367,000. Industrial production rose 0.1% for a second month, capacity utilization held at 76.1, and the University of Michigan's preliminary July consumer sentiment index jumped to 54.4 from 49.5 in June.
  • Starship's 13th test flight, Thursday July 23. SpaceX rescheduled the attempt after a Raptor 3 engine ignition failure triggered an automatic abort. TheStreet also notes SpaceX plans to rent out unused computing capacity from its Colossus data centre complex, per a Fortune report.
