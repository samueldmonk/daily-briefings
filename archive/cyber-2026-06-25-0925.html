Top Story

Operation Endgame dismantles the Amadey, StealC and SocGholish malware networks

Europol, Microsoft and a coalition of law-enforcement agencies and private partners disrupted the infrastructure behind three of the most prolific cybercrime tools — the Amadey loader/botnet, the StealC infostealer and the SocGholish fake-update framework — in the latest phase of Operation Endgame, announced June 24 after two weeks of coordinated action. Authorities and partners took down 326 servers and 142 domains; Microsoft's Digital Crimes Unit disrupted more than 200 command-and-control servers in a simultaneous, court-authorized takedown and filed a racketeering suit.

Europol said the action recovered 27 million stolen login credentials and identified and froze about €41 million ($46.5 million) in criminal cryptocurrency; Amadey and StealC alone were tied to more than 140,000 infected computers in the first two weeks of May 2026. The agency described the malware's roles plainly — "Amadey gains initial access to devices, while StealC extracts passwords and sensitive data." Participating countries included Canada, Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands, the United Kingdom and the United States, with private partners Microsoft, Proofpoint, IBM X-Force, Bitdefender, the Shadowserver Foundation and Have I Been Pwned.

Patch Priority

⚠ Ubiquiti UniFi OS — CVE-2026-34910 (CVSS 10.0) · Federal deadline June 26

CISA added four exploited flaws to the KEV catalog on June 23 — three max-severity (CVSS 10.0) Ubiquiti UniFi OS bugs (CVE-2026-34910/-34909/-34908) plus a Lantronix EDS5000 flaw — all confirmed actively exploited. CVE-2026-34910 is an improper-input-validation bug enabling command injection that researchers (Bishop Fox) showed can be chained to root-level remote code execution; early zero-day activity created rogue administrator accounts named "John Sim." Update to UniFi OS Server 5.0.8 or later (fix released May 21) per Ubiquiti's advisory before the June 26 federal due date. (The Splunk RCE in the KEV list below is already overdue — patch it now.)

Threat Actor Spotlight

KongTuke (aka Woodgnat)

An initial access broker active since at least 2024, KongTuke specializes in compromising corporate networks and selling that foothold to ransomware crews — reportedly including Qilin, Interlock, Rhysida, Akira, 8Base and Black Basta. This week researchers tied the broker to Mistic, a newly identified, memory-only backdoor (Zscaler tracks it as MTLBackdoor) seen since April against insurance, education, IT and professional-services targets and delivered through multi-stage "ClickFix" lures. Mistic writes nothing to disk and ships a self-delete kill switch — the kind of quiet, long-dwell access that typically precedes a ransomware deployment. The takeaway: even as Operation Endgame disrupts commodity infostealers, access brokers keep refreshing their toolkits.

Breaches & Incidents

Mistic backdoor / KongTukeNew

Researchers (Symantec; Zscaler tracks it as MTLBackdoor) detailed Mistic, a stealthy in-memory backdoor used since April against insurance, education, IT and professional-services firms. Linked to access broker KongTuke, it runs entirely in memory, writes no files to disk and self-deletes via a kill switch — built to hand ransomware crews quiet, persistent access.

Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN zero-dayNew

An unknown actor exploited CVE-2026-20245 (CVSS 7.8) in Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager as a zero-day for at least two months before public disclosure, running arbitrary commands with elevated privileges. CISA added the flaw to its KEV catalog on June 9; organizations should confirm they are on a fixed release.

Instructure / Canvas LMS

ShinyHunters breached the Canvas learning-management system and claimed data on as many as 275 million individuals across nearly 9,000 schools; after a reported May ransom payment the attackers resurfaced and defaced hundreds of school login screens during final exams.

Aflac

The insurer is notifying about 22.6 million people whose data was stolen in a Scattered Spider social-engineering attack first detected June 12, 2025; exposed data includes Social Security numbers and health information, with monitoring enrollment ongoing.

DentaQuest (Sun Life)

The U.S. dental-benefits administrator was hit after ShinyHunters leaked exfiltrated data; analysts assess about 2.6 million accounts were exposed, including names, emails, government IDs and health-insurance details.
