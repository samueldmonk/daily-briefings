The Lead

Stocks climb broadly after a cool jobs report eases rate-hike fears; Dow leads, small caps lag

A weaker-than-expected June employment report gave the bulls what they wanted on Thursday: relief from rate-hike anxiety. As of ~midday ET, all three major averages were higher — the Dow Jones Industrial Average led, up roughly 0.5%, the S&P 500 added about 0.3%–0.5%, and the Nasdaq Composite clawed back to modest gains as semiconductors steadied following Wednesday's rout. The clear laggard was the small-cap Russell 2000, off about 0.4%. Stocks opened higher after the data and broadened through the morning, with traders squaring positions ahead of the long holiday weekend (U.S. markets are shut Friday, July 3).

The catalyst: U.S. employers added just 57,000 jobs in June, well short of the roughly 115,000 economists expected and below a downwardly revised 129,000 in May, while April and May were cut by a combined 74,000. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2% (from 4.3%), but largely because the labor-force participation rate fell 0.3 point to 61.5%, its lowest since March 2021. Professional and business services (+36,000), social assistance (+25,000) and healthcare (+22,000) led the gains; leisure and hospitality shed 61,000. "For the Fed, this number is fine," said Jefferies economist Thomas Simons — and traders agreed, taking a September rate hike off the table (though CME FedWatch still prices a possible October move) under Chair Kevin Warsh, whose committee is holding the funds rate at 3.50%–3.75%.
