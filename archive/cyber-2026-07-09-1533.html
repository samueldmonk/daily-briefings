Top Story

FortiBleed credential theft is now a confirmed pipeline into INC and Lynx ransomware

Researchers tracking the FortiBleed campaign say a mass harvest of Fortinet FortiGate credentials has become a direct route into ransomware. The financially motivated operation collected more than 110 million credentials from roughly 430,000 internet-facing FortiGate firewalls, intercepting VPN logins and other authentication traffic directly from compromised appliances.

Investigators logged scanning against about 11,250 FortiGate portals in more than 150 countries, confirmed admin-level access on 409 targets and completed the full attack chain on 354 of them. One operator was found working both the INC Ransom and Lynx negotiation panels, and at least 12 ransomware deployments — encrypting hundreds of endpoints — have been tied to the access. The links surfaced after an operational-security slip left a FortiBleed server, holding credentials stolen from thousands of Fortinet appliances, exposed on the internet.

Threat-intel teams (SOCRadar, The Hacker News, DarkReading, SecurityWeek) call it the first confirmed instance connecting mass FortiGate credential theft to actual ransomware deployment; researchers estimate the crew runs roughly 20 members with defined roles. Defenders should treat any exposed FortiGate credentials as compromised: rotate all device and VPN credentials, enforce MFA, and apply Fortinet's latest FortiOS updates.

Patch Priority

Patch Adobe ColdFusion now — CVE-2026-48282 (CVSS 10.0) is actively exploited and the federal deadline is July 10

A maximum-severity path-traversal flaw in Adobe ColdFusion's Remote Development Services (RDS) FILEIO handler lets an unauthenticated attacker perform arbitrary file operations and upload a CFML webshell for full remote code execution as the ColdFusion service account. Adobe shipped a fix on June 30 with a Priority 1 rating, but researchers logged in-the-wild exploitation within under two hours of the technical details going public. CISA added it to the KEV catalog and set an FCEB remediation deadline of July 10, 2026 (one day out). Fix: apply Adobe's June 30 ColdFusion 2023/2025 updates immediately and hunt for webshells.

Threat Actor Spotlight

Qilin (a.k.a. Agenda)

The ransomware-as-a-service crew Qilin — also tracked as Agenda — is the most active ransomware operation of 2026, with researchers counting roughly 1,500 victims since launch and more than 500 in 2026 alone. It claimed 97 attacks in May (down about 10% from 108 in April) and leads the ecosystem, with manufacturing its most-targeted sector ahead of professional services and retail. Operators develop and maintain the infrastructure while affiliates carry out intrusions; recent victims tracked this period include the manufacturer Chemco. Its scale makes any Qilin note-drop a signal to check backups, segmentation and credential hygiene.

Breaches & Incidents

AssuranceAmerica breach exposes 6.9M driver's-license recordsNew

The U.S. auto insurer disclosed a data breach affecting nearly 7 million drivers after attackers accessed its systems earlier this year; about 6.9 million U.S. driver's-license records were exposed. Affected drivers should watch for identity-theft and phishing follow-ons.

Adobe ColdFusion max-severity flaw exploited within ~2 hoursExploited

Adobe's June 30 update fixed a CVSS 10.0 path-traversal flaw in ColdFusion (CVE-2026-48282) that yields unauthenticated remote code execution. Attackers began exploiting it within about two hours of public disclosure; it is now in CISA KEV with a July 10 federal deadline. Patch immediately.

Deutsche Bank breach claimed by a ransomware groupRansomwareNew

A ransomware group posted "evidence" of a claimed Deutsche Bank data breach on July 8. The claim is unverified by the bank at publication; treat the extortion post as alleged pending confirmation, and watch for follow-up leak-site activity.

Ford and Chemco named on ransomware leak sitesRansomware

Ford Motor Company was listed as a victim by the Krybit ransomware group, while manufacturer Chemco was claimed by Qilin. Separately, researchers flagged a new ransomware family, "GodDamn," that uses a kernel driver to disable security software.
