The Lead
Markets open · as of ~11:45 AM ET

Stocks claw back from a tech-led drop as rotation offsets the AI-cost sell-off — as of ~11:45 AM ET

U.S. stocks opened sharply lower Friday but pared the bulk of those losses by late morning. As of roughly 11:45 AM ET the S&P 500 had clawed back to near the flatline, the Nasdaq Composite had trimmed an early ~1.3% drop to about 0.2%, and the Dow outperformed — little changed to higher — helped by a rotation into industrials and health care, while the small-cap Russell 2000 held a gain of about 0.7%. The morning's trigger: mounting worries over the cost of building out artificial-intelligence infrastructure, amplified by a report that OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its IPO to 2027 as it struggles to lock in demand at a roughly $1 trillion valuation. In Tokyo, SoftBank tumbled about 12%, leading a broad rout in Asian tech that closed South Korea's Kospi down 5.8% after an 8% intraday drop tripped a circuit breaker.

The backdrop is a market on its back foot: on Thursday the Nasdaq Composite fell a fourth straight session — its first four-day losing streak since February — to 25,358.60, while the S&P 500 finished essentially flat at 7,357.49 and the Dow eked out a 0.14% gain to 51,920.62 after tagging a record intraday high. Under the surface, Friday's tape shows a clear, healthier rotation: with six of 11 S&P sectors green, industrials (≈ +2.2%), health care (≈ +1.5%) and materials (≈ +1.4%) are leading even as megacaps slide — Apple ≈ −6.1%, Microsoft ≈ −3.5%, Amazon ≈ −3.1%, Meta ≈ −2.7%, Nvidia ≈ −1.6%. Crude kept sliding — WTI back below $70, near pre-war levels, as more tankers move through the Strait of Hormuz — pulling the 10-year Treasury yield to its lowest in more than six weeks. Thursday's hot May PCE print (headline ~4.1%, core ~3.4% year-over-year) keeps a possible 2026 Fed rate hike in the conversation.
