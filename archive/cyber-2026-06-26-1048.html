Top Story

Operation Endgame strikes again — StealC, Amadey and SocGholish networks dismantled

Law EnforcementMalware TakedownEuropol

Europol and Eurojust announced on June 24 that the latest phase of Operation Endgame neutralized 326 servers and 142 domains behind three of the world's most prolific malware loaders and infostealers — StealC, Amadey and SocGholish. Investigators recovered roughly 27 million stolen credentials (about 25.6 million unique) from more than 385,000 compromised systems and froze over €41 million (about $46.5 million) in criminal crypto assets.

Law-enforcement agencies from Canada, Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands, the UK and the US took part, alongside private partners including Microsoft, Bitdefender, BitSight, ESET, IBM X-Force, Lumen, Proofpoint and Mitsui Bussan Secure Directions. Amadey and StealC — which work in tandem, with Amadey gaining initial access and StealC harvesting passwords — were linked to more than 140,000 infected computers in the first two weeks of May alone. The action also struck the infrastructure behind SocGholish, the long-running "fake browser update" operation.

Ubiquiti UniFi OS — CVE-2026-34910 (CVSS 10.0)

An improper-input-validation flaw lets an attacker inject and run arbitrary OS commands. Bishop Fox showed it chains with two other max-severity bugs — CVE-2026-34908 (access-control bypass) and CVE-2026-34909 (path traversal) — for unauthenticated, root-level remote code execution on UniFi OS devices. The flaws are being exploited in the wild, with researchers reporting Mirai-style botnet activity against exposed devices. Fix: apply Ubiquiti's UniFi OS security updates (released in May). CISA added all three to the KEV catalog on June 23 with a federal remediation deadline of June 26, 2026 under BOD 26-04 — that deadline is today; patch now.

Threat Actor Spotlight

KongTuke — the ransomware access broker behind ‘Mistic’

Researchers at Symantec and Carbon Black's Threat Hunter Team tied the stealthy Mistic backdoor — which Zscaler ThreatLabz first documented as MLTBackdoor — to KongTuke, an initial-access broker active since at least 2024 and also tracked as Woodgnat. KongTuke compromises corporate networks and sells that foothold to ransomware crews, with access reportedly funneled to Qilin, Interlock, Rhysida, Akira, 8Base and Black Basta. Mistic has been delivered through multi-stage "ClickFix" lures and dropped alongside ModeloRAT, a Python RAT KongTuke spreads via Microsoft Teams social engineering — a reminder that today's quiet intrusion is often tomorrow's ransomware deployment.

Breaches & Incidents

Cisco Unified CM exploited in the wild

CISA added CVE-2026-20230, a server-side request forgery flaw in Cisco Unified Communications Manager, to its KEV catalog on June 25 after confirmed exploitation. Attackers chain it to write arbitrary files to the underlying OS and escalate to root, with researchers reporting web shells dropped on compromised call-manager servers weeks after Cisco's patch shipped. Federal agencies must remediate by June 28. CISA added a critical PTC Windchill/FlexPLM RCE (CVE-2026-12569, deserialization of untrusted data) the same day.

‘Mistic’ fileless backdoor campaign

Symantec and Carbon Black detailed a stealthy backdoor, Mistic (aka MLTBackdoor), in suspected financially-motivated intrusions against insurance, education, IT and professional-services organizations since April 2026. It runs payloads entirely in memory — nothing touches disk — and carries a self-delete kill switch; Zscaler found roughly 95% of its code is junk math meant to defeat automated analysis. It is linked to access broker KongTuke and dropped alongside the ModeloRAT Python trojan.

‘The Gentlemen’ ransomware claims Thai tech firm

CYFIRMA reported that The Gentlemen ransomware operation listed a leading Bangkok-based technology provider (founded 1971) on its dark-web leak site. The crew runs a dual-extortion, self-propagating Go encryptor (ChaCha20-Poly1305) detailed by Microsoft, recruits affiliates through BreachForums on a 90/10 split, and has been hitting critical-sector targets across multiple regions.

Klue — Salesforce supply-chain theft

A threat actor calling itself Icarus used a compromised legacy credential at competitive-intelligence platform Klue to mint OAuth tokens and siphon Salesforce CRM data from Klue's customers. LastPass confirmed support-case data was accessed (products, infrastructure and vaults unaffected); BeyondTrust, 8x8 and Pendo also disclosed exposure, and security vendors including HackerOne, Huntress, Snyk and Tanium were in the blast radius. Klue disclosed the June 11–12 intrusion on June 19; tokens have been rotated.
