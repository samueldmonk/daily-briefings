The Lead

Wall Street pares a chip-driven slide — the Dow edges into the green and the S&P trims its loss to ~1%, even as Micron-led semiconductors keep the Nasdaq down ~1.5% (as of ~1 PM ET)

U.S. stocks clawed back a chunk of their morning losses by early Tuesday afternoon, even as a worldwide technology selloff that began Monday kept memory-chip shares routed on fresh doubts about stretched AI and semiconductor valuations. The Nasdaq Composite was off about 1.5% — well off an early plunge of nearly 3% — and the S&P 500 trimmed its drop to roughly 1% (−0.97%), while the Dow Jones Industrial Average edged into the green, up about 0.25%, as the damage stayed concentrated in chips. Micron sank about 11% to $1,074.60 ahead of its fiscal-Q3 earnings Wednesday, SanDisk tumbled about 11.7% to $2,006.99, Western Digital fell about 8.4% to $671, Qualcomm about 6.9% to $206.60, Arm Holdings about 8.4% and Taiwan Semiconductor 5.2% to $443.35, while the VanEck Semiconductor ETF dropped about 6.5% to $625.62 and Nvidia slid about 3.2% to $201.97. The selling spread from Asia, where South Korea's Kospi plunged 9.99% — its steepest drop in more than three months — triggering a 20-minute trading halt as Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix each tumbled more than 12%, and Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.55%.

Beneath the chip carnage, breadth held up far better than the headline tech damage suggested. With the damage concentrated in semiconductors, defensive and software names led the rebound — Public Storage rose about 4.4% to $334.43, IBM about 4.2% to $263 and Accenture about 3.3% to $128.82, while megacaps Microsoft (+2.5%) and Amazon (+1.7%) and drugmaker Merck (+2.4%) also bucked the selloff — enough to lift the Dow into positive territory even as Nvidia (−3.2%) and Tesla (−4.2%) dragged on the Nasdaq. (Super Micro had been the standout on Monday, surging roughly 16% on NVIDIA's Vera Rubin platform reveal at ISC High Performance 2026 — where it was named alongside Dell to build custom NVL4 racks — plus a GF Securities upgrade, before getting caught in Tuesday's broad tech weakness.) Oracle slipped about 2.6% to $170.85 after disclosing it cut roughly 21,000 jobs over the past year as it retools around AI. Oil stayed soft — WTI traded near $73.67 on Tuesday, easing from Monday's $74.82 settle on reported U.S.–Iran progress — while the 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.5%, its highest in about two weeks, after last week's more hawkish Fed under new Chair Kevin Warsh. Investors are looking ahead to FedEx earnings after Tuesday's close and Thursday's May PCE inflation report, the week's key macro test.
