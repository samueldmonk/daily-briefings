Top Story

"FortiBleed" campaign still controls credentials for ~86,600 Fortinet firewalls across 194 countries

Researchers continue to track a large-scale, fully automated campaign — dubbed FortiBleed by SOCRadar — that has harvested working login credentials from internet-facing Fortinet FortiGate firewall and SSL-VPN devices worldwide. An attacker-controlled database holds valid credentials for more than 86,600 FortiGate devices (SOCRadar's count; SecurityWeek reports ~86,000 and Recorded Future tallies 73,932) spanning 194 countries per Arctic Wolf — roughly half of all Fortinet firewalls reachable on the internet — belonging to companies and government organizations.

The operation works in two stages: attackers first reuse usernames and passwords assembled from earlier Fortinet breach dumps and infostealer-malware logs, testing them automatically against internet-facing FortiGate devices around the clock; a compromised firewall is then used as a passive listening post that skims further credentials from the SSL-VPN traffic passing through it, feeding them back into the scanner. The campaign has been running since at least February 2026 and remains active. Fortinet characterizes the activity as credential reuse and brute-forcing rather than a new product vulnerability; CISA's June 18 advisory urges organizations to rotate FortiGate and VPN credentials, enforce MFA, and upgrade firmware. SOCRadar's tooling and targeting are consistent with Russian-speaking threat actors.

Patch Priority

Patch Ubiquiti UniFi OS now — CVE-2026-34908 / -34909 / -34910 (CVSS 10.0) are exploited; federal deadline June 26

The single most urgent action for defenders today is the max-severity (CVSS 10.0) Ubiquiti UniFi OS trio — an improper-access-control flaw (CVE-2026-34908) plus a path-traversal (CVE-2026-34909) and an improper-input-validation bug (CVE-2026-34910) — that CISA added to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on June 23 based on evidence of active exploitation. They are remotely exploitable by an unauthenticated attacker. Under BOD 26-04, federal agencies must apply Ubiquiti's fixes or discontinue affected UniFi OS products by June 26, 2026 (two days out). Apply the vendor updates (Security Advisory Bulletin 064) immediately. Note: the actively-exploited Splunk RCE (CVE-2026-20253, CVSS 9.8) is also still overdue — patch it if you have not.

Threat Actor Spotlight

ShinyHunters — data-theft & extortion crew (tracked by Mandiant as UNC6240)

The extortion group ShinyHunters — which Google's Mandiant tracks as UNC6240 — exploited a zero-day in Oracle PeopleSoft (CVE-2026-35273, an unauthenticated PeopleTools RCE rated 9.8) to breach more than 100 organizations across 300-plus instances. Mandiant dates the activity to May 27–June 9, before Oracle published its advisory on June 10, and says about 68% of victims were in higher education, most of them in the United States. Have I Been Pwned has counted roughly 455,000 unique email addresses in the leaked data, covering current students and alumni; the University of Nottingham is among the first confirmed victims. The group runs a pure data-theft-and-extortion model — no file-encrypting ransomware.

Breaches & Incidents

Oracle PeopleSoft — universities hit hardest New

ShinyHunters' exploitation of PeopleSoft zero-day CVE-2026-35273 has exposed hundreds of thousands of student and alumni records — names, addresses, phone numbers, passport numbers and sensitive demographic data — at 100+ organizations, ~68% of them in higher education. Have I Been Pwned counts ~455,000 unique emails.

Klue → Salesforce / Gong theft

Marketing-intelligence platform Klue confirmed an attacker used a compromised legacy credential to obtain OAuth access tokens and steal data directly from Klue customers' Salesforce and Gong instances; the emerging "Icarus" crew has claimed the campaign and begun leaking data.

ShapedPlugin WordPress supply-chain backdoor New

Multiple WordPress plugins from ShapedPlugin were compromised in a supply-chain attack after unknown threat actors tampered with the official release channels and pushed backdoor code to downstream sites.

Tchap — French government messaging app New

France's government messaging application Tchap was hacked; a threat actor claims to have stolen 13.5 GB of data, including 73,467 user accounts tied to French ministries, 643,459 messages, and hundreds of chat rooms with history.
