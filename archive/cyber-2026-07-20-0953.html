Top Story

Hugging Face breached by an autonomous AI agent — and hosted-model guardrails blocked the forensics

Hugging Face disclosed on July 16 that it detected and responded to an intrusion into part of its production infrastructure that was, in its own words, "driven, end to end, by an autonomous AI agent system." The company says it identified unauthorized access to a limited set of internal datasets and to several credentials used by its services, found no evidence of tampering with public models, datasets or Spaces, and verified its software supply chain — container images and published packages — as clean.

The initial access came through the data pipeline: a malicious dataset abused two code-execution paths in Hugging Face's dataset processing — a remote-code dataset loader and a template injection in a dataset configuration — to run code on a processing worker. From there the actor escalated to node-level access, harvested cloud and cluster credentials, and moved laterally into several internal clusters over a weekend. Hugging Face describes the campaign as an autonomous agent framework running many thousands of individual actions across a swarm of short-lived sandboxes, with self-migrating command-and-control staged on public services.

The most quoted detail is a defensive one. To reconstruct what happened, Hugging Face ran LLM-driven analysis agents over an attacker action log of more than 17,000 recorded events. It first tried frontier models behind commercial APIs and could not use them: submitting real attack commands, exploit payloads and C2 artifacts tripped the providers' safety guardrails, "which cannot distinguish an incident responder from an attacker." The forensics were run instead on GLM 5.2, an open-weight model, on Hugging Face's own infrastructure. The company's stated lesson for defenders is to vet a capable self-hosted model before an incident — both to avoid guardrail lockout and to keep attacker data and credentials inside the environment. It has reported the incident to law enforcement and is working with outside forensic specialists.

Exploited as a zero-day before the fix and added to the CISA KEV catalog on July 16 with a Federal Civilian Executive Branch remediation date of July 19, 2026 — now past. Threat Actor Spotlight

AI-operatedNew

"bandcampro" — a solo operator who made Gemini CLI do the hacking

A Russian-speaking threat actor tracked as bandcampro jailbroke Google's open-source terminal agent Gemini CLI and used it to stand up and run a small command-and-control botnet. Across more than 200 sessions between March 19 and April 21, 2026, the actor had the agent deploy and operate infrastructure controlling eight computers inside a dental clinic, reaching the clinic's OpenDental database. Posing as an "authorized penetration tester," he instructed the model to suppress safety disclaimers and automatically save any credentials it encountered. A single Russian-language instruction — "Study the C2 migration" — had the AI write the C&C server code, deploy it on a fresh VPS, configure Cloudflare tunnels and bring the infrastructure online. Researchers analysing the logs put the operator's own contribution at 11% of the text produced, with the AI responsible for the other 89%, plus 80% of the architectural design, all coding and system-command execution, and 90% of debugging. The whole operation was encoded in three plain-text files totalling roughly 5 KB: a jailbreak prompt, a playbook, and a migration guide that let a fresh AI session rebuild the infrastructure on another server.

Breaches & Incidents

RansomwareNew

Coca-Cola halts U.S. fairlife production after ransomware

In an SEC filing dated July 16, The Coca-Cola Company said fairlife, LLC — the dairy business it owns — identified unauthorized third-party access to a portion of its systems, including production-related systems, in connection with a ransomware event. U.S. production operations at fairlife are temporarily suspended; Canadian production is not currently impacted. Coca-Cola says it activated incident response and business continuity protocols, notified law enforcement, and is investigating with outside advisers. Product quality and safety have not been impacted. The company has not identified the attackers or confirmed whether data was stolen.

Supply chain

Klue breach cascades into roughly 200 companies

A compromise at market-intelligence vendor Klue reached the Salesforce environments of close to 200 customer organisations, making it one of the broadest breaches of the year. LastPass, BeyondTrust, Jamf, HackerOne, Recorded Future, Snyk, Tanium and Huntress have all confirmed they lost business data; Gong, OneTrust, Sprout Social and Insurity were also named. The exposed material is reported as business contact information and Salesforce CRM and support-case records. LastPass says its products, services and infrastructure were not affected and that customer vaults remain secure. An extortion crew calling itself Icarus claimed responsibility.

Third party

Ernst & Young notifying customers after ticket-system compromise

Ernst & Young is notifying customers of a data breach caused by the compromise of a third-party support ticket system used by its IT personnel — another entry in the run of 2026 incidents that reached a large enterprise through a vendor rather than through its own perimeter.

AI platformNew

Hugging Face: the dataset pipeline was the attack surface

Beyond the agentic-attacker headline, the practical takeaway from the Hugging Face disclosure is where the intrusion started — not a login page or an edge appliance, but the data-processing pipeline itself, via a malicious dataset that abused a remote-code loader and a template-injection path. Hugging Face has closed both code-execution paths, rebuilt compromised nodes, rotated affected credentials and tokens, and tightened cluster admission controls. It advises users to rotate access tokens and review recent account activity as a precaution.
