The Lead

Wall Street closes lower as a deepening chip rout sinks the Nasdaq ~2.2% and the S&P ~1.3% — but the Dow ekes out a small gain as a BofA call for three 2026 Fed hikes lifts yields (closing bell, June 23)

U.S. stocks gave back an afternoon rebound and slid into Tuesday's close, as a worldwide technology selloff that began Monday kept memory-chip shares routed on fresh doubts about stretched AI and semiconductor valuations. The Nasdaq Composite finished down about 2.2%, closing near 25,587 (off 579 points) — a deeper loss than its mid-afternoon −1.5% as selling resumed in the final hour — while the S&P 500 fell roughly 1.3%. The Dow Jones Industrial Average, which holds fewer tech names, eked out a small gain of about 0.1%, as the damage stayed concentrated in chips, and the Russell 2000 slipped about 0.8%, dropping back below 3,000 a day after its first-ever close above that line. Micron led the rout, down as much as 11.4% to $1,074.60 (paring to roughly −10%) ahead of its fiscal-Q3 earnings Wednesday, while SanDisk tumbled about 11.7%, Western Digital fell about 8.4%, Qualcomm about 6.9%, Arm Holdings about 8.4% and Taiwan Semiconductor 5.2%, the VanEck Semiconductor ETF dropped about 6.5% and Nvidia slid about 3.2%. The selling spread from Asia, where South Korea's Kospi plunged about 10% — its steepest drop in more than three months — triggering a 20-minute trading halt as Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix each tumbled more than 12%, and Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.55%. The Cboe Volatility Index (VIX) jumped about 12% to roughly 19.

Beneath the chip carnage, breadth held up far better than the headline tech damage suggested. With the damage concentrated in semiconductors, defensive and software names led the green — IBM rose about 5% (near $265) after a JPMorgan upgrade to overweight, Public Storage about 4.4% to $334.43 and Accenture about 3.3% to $128.82, while megacaps Microsoft (+2.5%) and Amazon (+1.7%) and drugmaker Merck (+2.4%) also bucked the selloff — enough to lift the Dow into positive territory even as Nvidia (−3.2%) and Tesla (−4.2%) dragged on the Nasdaq. Oracle slipped about 2.6% to $170.85 after disclosing it cut roughly 21,000 jobs over the past year as it retools around AI. Adding to the pressure, Bank of America became the latest bank to flip hawkish, reversing its forecast to call for three quarter-point Fed rate hikes in 2026 — in September, October and December — that would lift the target range to 4.25%–4.5%, warning that "the Fed's inflation problem has gotten unambiguously worse" under new Chair Kevin Warsh. Treasury yields climbed in response: the 10-year held near 4.5%, its highest in about two weeks, and the 2-year rose above 4.2%, its highest since February 2025, while futures pushed the odds of a September hike to roughly 70%+ (CME FedWatch), up from about 29% a week ago. Oil stayed soft — WTI traded near $74 (Brent near $78), easing on reported U.S.–Iran progress after Washington granted Tehran a 60-day oil-sales waiver. Investors now turn to FedEx and Cerebras earnings after Tuesday's close, Micron on Wednesday and Thursday's May PCE inflation report, the week's key macro test.
