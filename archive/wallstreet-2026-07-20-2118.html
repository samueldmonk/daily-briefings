The Lead

Iran worries derail the S&P and the Dow; a chip comeback rescues the Nasdaq

The bell has rung. Monday, July 20 closed mixed-to-lower. The S&P 500 finished at 7,443.28, down 0.19%. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 307.16 points, or 0.59%, to 51,839.26. The Nasdaq Composite was the standout, essentially flat at 25,508.07, down just 0.05%. CNBC attributed the S&P’s decline to advancing oil prices in response to the latest military exchanges between the US and Iran.

All three closes reconcile exactly to Friday’s verified levels — 7,457.69, 25,520.24 and 52,146.42 — on both points and percentage, which is the arithmetic check this page applies before publishing any level.

The shape of the day was a fade. Stocks opened firmer: index futures were higher pre-bell (S&P futures +0.35%, Dow futures +0.23%, Nasdaq futures +0.76% on a 7:24 AM Yahoo Finance capture) as semiconductors tried to recover from a punishing week. That bid held long enough for the Nasdaq to finish nearly unchanged, but it could not carry the broader tape: TheStreet headlined the session “Iran worries derail Nasdaq, S&P 500 despite modest chip comeback,” and Trading Economics recorded that US indices “closed mostly lower… as gains in technology stocks failed to offset weakness in the broader market amid concerns over further escalation in the Middle East.”

The macro backdrop hardened through the day. The US conducted a new round of airstrikes against Iran and reported the death of a third American service member, killed in northern Iraq during the controlled detonation of a downed Iranian drone. Iran’s Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei said negotiations could still be pursued if they align with Iran’s national interests, and Iranian state agency IRNA reported that mediators had put proposals to Tehran — enough to pull Brent back after it briefly cleared $90. Treasury yields rose with energy-driven inflation concerns: the 10-year closed at 4.598%, up 0.057.

Attention now shifts to earnings. Capital.com senior market analyst Daniela Hathorn: “Earnings season gathers pace with reports from Tesla, Alphabet, Intel and several major industrial and consumer companies. After last week’s sharp rotation out of AI-related stocks, investors will be looking for evidence that earnings can continue to justify elevated valuations.” She added that with “geopolitics, central bank expectations and corporate earnings all competing for attention, markets are likely to remain highly headline-driven throughout the week.”

Movers & Drivers

+11%NewData centers

Hut 8 — a $9.8 billion lease

Hut 8 Corp popped 11% after signing a 15-year lease with an existing customer that fully commercializes its 1-gigawatt Beacon Point data center in Texas. CNBC put the total value of the agreement at $9.8 billion — one of the cleanest single-catalyst moves on the board in a session otherwise driven by macro headlines.

+5%NewAI

Alibaba — Qwen 3.8 Max preview

Alibaba’s US-listed shares rose 5% after the company previewed a new large AI model, Qwen 3.8 Max, which Alibaba said is second only to Anthropic’s Fable 5 model. The move ran counter to the broader caution around AI valuations that has dominated the last two weeks of tape.

−8%NewRestaurants

Sweetgreen — cyclospora investigation

Sweetgreen shares fell 8% on the day, with Cava Group also trading lower, as the FDA continued to investigate a cyclospora outbreak. A rare instance this session of a genuinely company-specific risk rather than a macro one.

+2%NewSemis

Chips claw back — SOXX +2% midday

The iShares Semiconductor ETF (SOXX) rose 2% in midday trading after tumbling 10% last week — the modest comeback that kept the Nasdaq Composite within 0.05% of unchanged. Context from Friday: the PHLX Semiconductor index had closed more than 20% below its late-June record, confirming a bear market in the group.

HigherEarningsReadings disputed

Domino’s Pizza — revenue beat, EPS miss

Q2 revenue of $1.19 billion, up 4.3% year over year, edged the $1.18 billion consensus, while diluted EPS of $4.07 came in under the $4.17 expected; same-store sales also fell short. The stock rose, but how much is not settled: readings this run ranged from +8.3% premarket (GuruFocus) to +6.5% at $343.00 intraday (Schaeffer’s) to +7.2% and separately “more than 1%” in two different CNBC renders. Direction is certain; the magnitude is published here as a range rather than a single figure.

+1.62%PremarketSpace

SpaceX — Starship test flight reset to Thursday

SPCX rose 1.62% to $125.99 in premarket trading. SpaceX has rescheduled the 13th test flight of Starship for Thursday, July 23, after a Raptor 3 engine ignition failure forced an automatic abort of the earlier attempt. Its June 12 IPO remains the largest in history, raising $75 billion and briefly pushing the valuation above $2 trillion; the stock hit an all-time intraday high of $225.64 three trading days later. Separately, Fortune reported the company plans to rent out unused computing capacity from its Colossus data center complex.

On the Radar

  • Big Tech earnings, all week. Alphabet, Intel, IBM and Tesla report. Wall Street has raised its expectations for these results, and after the recent rotation out of AI-linked names investors are looking for evidence that companies are monetizing AI to accompany their build-out spending.
  • FOMC, July 28–29. Policymakers are expected to leave the target range at 3.50–3.75% while preserving the option to tighten further if inflation proves persistent. This is the second meeting under Chair Kevin Warsh.
  • US–Iran. The conflict is now the dominant macro input: fresh airstrikes, a third American service member killed, and Brent briefly above $90 before mediator-driven negotiation headlines pulled it back. Yahoo Finance notes Brent remains well below its April and May peaks, suggesting energy markets may be starting to look beyond the Strait of Hormuz to alternative shipping routes.
