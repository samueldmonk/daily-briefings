The Lead

Stocks close mixed — then Micron's blowout steals the after-hours show (June 24)

Wall Street ended a choppy, mixed session little changed on Wednesday. A morning bounce faded into the afternoon, leaving the Dow modestly higher while the S&P 500 and Nasdaq Composite finished slightly lower, as traders held fire ahead of the day's main event — Micron's fiscal-Q3 report after the bell. The split reflected rotation more than direction: tumbling oil (WTI settled near $70, a pre-war low) pulled Treasury yields down and supported cyclicals, while semiconductors stayed soft into the print. (Final index levels were still being confirmed at publication; the most recent official close — Tuesday's — is in the Weekly Scorecard.)

Then Micron delivered a blowout. After the close, the memory maker reported record fiscal-third-quarter revenue of $41.46 billion — up from $9.30 billion a year earlier and well above Street estimates near $35 billion — with non-GAAP earnings of $25.11 per share (versus roughly $20 expected) and a record non-GAAP gross margin of 84.9%; operating cash flow hit $25.39 billion and the board declared a $0.15 dividend. More striking still was the guidance: fiscal-Q4 revenue of about $50.0 billion (±$1.0B), an ~86% gross margin and non-GAAP EPS near $31.00 — figures that blew past expectations. CEO Sanjay Mehrotra credited new multi-year "Strategic Customer Agreements" for adding durability, noting HBM capacity is sold out through 2026 and that HBM4 is shipping in volume to its lead customer. Shares climbed in after-hours trading. The print follows Tuesday's deep, chip-led selloff, when the Nasdaq Composite closed −2.21% at 25,587.04, the S&P 500 fell 1.44% to 7,365.46, the Dow finished essentially flat (−0.09%) at 51,666.84, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index dropped more than 8%.

Within the regular session, the eye-catching mover was Wendy's (WEN), up more than 40% intraday on a "Save Wendy's" retail-meme short squeeze and the hire of Steve Cirulis (ex-Potbelly CFO/chief strategy officer) as CFO and chief strategy officer. The day's standout tech story was in custom silicon: Broadcom climbed after it and OpenAI unveiled "Jalapeño," OpenAI's first in-house AI chip — an LLM-inference accelerator built by Broadcom that CEO Hock Tan says runs roughly 50% cheaper than typical GPUs, with initial deployment targeted for late 2026. Sunrun (+22%) jumped on a data-center power deal that reframes solar as an AI-infrastructure play, while Hertz fell ~27% on weak Q2 guidance and a ~$100M stock offering, and Cerebras (−16%) slid in its post-IPO debut on a soft margin outlook, with Casey's (−7%) also lower. In an index shake-up, Alphabet (GOOGL) joins the Dow before Monday, June 29's open, replacing Verizon. On the macro side, the Fed held its target at 3.50–3.75% on June 17 — a fourth straight hold and the first meeting under new Chair Kevin Warsh — and BofA and Deutsche Bank now expect three quarter-point hikes in 2026, with futures pricing roughly a 68% chance of a September hike. WTI crude settled near $70.11 (−4.2%) — its lowest since early March, before the U.S.–Iran conflict — as oil tankers began transiting the Strait of Hormuz amid easing Middle East tensions; the slide left the 10-year Treasury yield around 4.48%, while gold eased ~1.4%.

Movers & Drivers

Micron — record quarter, ~$50B guide ▲ after hours

The marquee catalyst delivered: fiscal-Q3 revenue of $41.46B and $25.11 non-GAAP EPS (vs ~$20 expected), a record 84.9% gross margin, and guidance for ~$50.0B revenue and ~$31 EPS next quarter on an ~86% margin. HBM is sold out through 2026 and HBM4 is shipping in volume; new multi-year customer agreements underpin the outlook. Shares rose after the bell.

Wendy's — meme squeeze meets CFO hire ▲ 40%+ intraday

The day's biggest single regular-session mover. Wendy's surged more than 40% before paring after naming Steve Cirulis — formerly CFO/chief strategy officer at Potbelly — to the same roles, a catalyst amplified by a "Save Wendy's" retail-meme campaign targeting the heavily shorted shares.

Broadcom — OpenAI's "Jalapeño" chip ▲

Broadcom rose after it and OpenAI unveiled "Jalapeño," OpenAI's first custom AI accelerator, built by Broadcom for LLM inference. CEO Hock Tan said it runs roughly 50% cheaper than typical GPUs; OpenAI says it was designed end-to-end in about nine months, with first deployments targeted for late 2026 — a direct challenge to Nvidia in inference.

Decliners — Hertz, Cerebras, Casey's ▼

Hertz (HTZ) fell about 27% after guiding Q2 adjusted core earnings toward the low end of its range and announcing a ~$100M stock offering; AI-chip designer Cerebras (CBRS) dropped ~16% in its post-IPO debut on a soft full-year margin outlook; and Casey's (CASY) lost ~7% — pockets of weakness against an otherwise rotation-driven tape.
