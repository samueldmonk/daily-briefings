Top Story

A code-protection vendor's npm package shipped a credential stealer — and --ignore-scripts doesn't save you

Jscrambler sells JavaScript code protection. On July 11, 2026 its own npm package, at version 8.14.0, began installing a Rust infostealer — a preinstall hook that dropped and executed a native binary, with builds for Windows, macOS and Linux. Socket flagged the release six minutes after it went up, and now attributes five malicious versions to the same actor, pushed over roughly three hours: 8.14.0, 8.16.0, 8.17.0, 8.18.0 and 8.20.0. Jscrambler has confirmed the cause — a compromised npm publishing credential.

The detail that makes this worth leading with is the escalation mid-campaign. In 8.14.0, 8.16.0 and 8.17.0 the malware ran from the preinstall hook. In 8.18.0 and 8.20.0 the dropper was moved into the package's main code and CLI, so it fires when the package is imported or run — meaning the standard defensive reflex, npm install --ignore-scripts, does not stop it. Anyone who reached for that mitigation and moved on should re-check.

The stealer's target list reads like an inventory of a modern developer's machine: cloud credentials for AWS, GCP and Azure; crypto wallets including MetaMask, Trust Wallet, Coinbase Wallet, Phantom and Exodus; AI coding assistants — Claude Desktop, Cursor, Windsurf, Factory, Zed and VS Code; Discord, Slack and Telegram Desktop; browsers, gaming platforms and OS keyrings. Jscrambler has revoked and rotated its publishing credentials, hardened its pipeline and deprecated the bad releases — but deprecation still leaves them installable by exact version. Both Jscrambler and Socket point to 8.22.0 as the clean release.

Patch Priority

Do this first — deadline passed

SonicWall SMA1000: CVE-2026-15409 & CVE-2026-15410 — federal due date was July 17

The SonicWall SMA1000 pair remains the single most urgent item on this page. CVE-2026-15409 (server-side request forgery, CVSS 10.0) chains with CVE-2026-15410 (code injection); both were added to the CISA KEV catalog on July 14 with a stated remediation date of July 17, 2026 — now 3 days overdue.

Volexity reports an actor it tracks as UTA0533 was exploiting these as zero-days since June 22, 2026, roughly three weeks before public disclosure. Treat any internet-facing SMA1000 as compromised-until-proven-otherwise rather than merely unpatched: patch, then hunt.

Threat Actor Spotlight

UTA0533 — purpose-built tooling for one appliance

A previously undocumented actor, tracked by Volexity researchers Sean Koessel and Steven Adair, exploited the SonicWall SMA1000 zero-days from June 22, 2026 — about three weeks before the July 14 disclosure. The group used multiple zero-days alongside malware built specifically for SonicWall SMA appliances, which points at deliberate, sustained investment in a single edge product rather than opportunistic scanning. This page does not assert that UTA0533 and the Inc ransomware affiliate reported by Rapid7 are the same operator — that link is not established in the reporting reviewed.

Breaches & Incidents

RansomwareProduction halted

Ransomware has stopped U.S. production at Coca-Cola's fairlife

On July 16, 2026 The Coca-Cola Company disclosed in a Form 8-K that fairlife, LLC identified unauthorized third-party access to a portion of its systems — including production-related systems — in connection with a ransomware event. U.S. production operations are temporarily suspended; Canadian production is not currently impacted. Coca-Cola activated incident response and business continuity protocols, is investigating with outside advisers, and has notified law enforcement; it says product quality and safety have not been impacted. Still unknown: Coca-Cola has not identified the attackers and has not confirmed whether data was stolen, and no ransomware operation had publicly claimed responsibility at disclosure. The instructive detail is that encryption reached production systems rather than corporate IT alone — which is what turns an intrusion into a stopped manufacturing line.

Breach

Ernst & Young: client tax records taken via a third-party help desk

EY is notifying clients after the compromise of a third-party support-ticket platform used by its IT personnel. EY spotted anomalous activity on April 23, 2026, but its investigation determined the unauthorized party had access earlier — between March 28 and April 12, 2026 — and downloaded documents relating to a number of clients. Because support tickets carried attachments, the exposure includes Social Security numbers, financial account codes, credit and debit account data, investment holdings and the contents of client tax filings. EY filed with the California Attorney General on July 15, with regulatory filings now in four states, and is offering 24 months of Experian identity monitoring (enrolment by October 31, 2026).

AI securityOngoing

Hugging Face: an intrusion run end to end by an autonomous agent

Disclosed July 16, and still the most consequential item on this page even as fresher stories lead. The world's largest AI model repository says an intrusion into part of its production infrastructure was "driven, end to end, by an autonomous AI agent system." Initial access came via a malicious dataset abusing two code-execution paths — a remote-code dataset loader and template injection in a dataset configuration — giving node-level access on a processing worker, then credential harvest and lateral movement across several internal clusters over a weekend. A limited set of internal datasets and several service credentials were accessed; there is no evidence of tampering with public models, datasets or Spaces, and the container/package supply chain verified clean. Forensics covered more than 17,000 recorded events — and frontier commercial APIs refused the analysis on safety guardrails that "cannot distinguish an incident responder from an attacker," so Hugging Face ran it on self-hosted open-weight GLM 5.2. Users should rotate access tokens.

Abuse of AI toolingNew

"bandcampro" ran a botnet through Google's Gemini CLI

A Russian-speaking threat actor tracked as bandcampro used Google's Gemini CLI as the control layer for a botnet of eight dental-clinic PCs. The findings come from analysis of roughly 200 Gemini CLI session logs, which show the tooling used to crack passwords and stand up further operations. Small in scale and unglamorous in target — which is rather the point: the interesting variable is a general-purpose agentic CLI being pressed into service as attacker infrastructure.
