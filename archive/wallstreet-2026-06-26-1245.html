The Lead
Markets open · as of ~12:30 PM ET

Stocks hover near flat as a defensive rotation offsets the AI-cost sell-off — as of ~12:30 PM ET

U.S. stocks opened sharply lower Friday and clawed most of the way back by midday. As of roughly 12:30 PM ET the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq Composite were hovering near the flatline — each having pared an early drop of more than 1% — while the Dow Jones Industrial Average traded little changed and the small-cap Russell 2000 outperformed, up about 0.7%. The driver was a rotation out of technology and into defensives — health care, industrials and consumer staples. Even so, the major indexes are still tracking a losing week, with the S&P down more than 1% and the Nasdaq off about 4%, though the Dow is pacing for a roughly 0.6% weekly gain. The morning's trigger: mounting worries over the cost of building out artificial-intelligence infrastructure, amplified by a report that OpenAI is leaning toward delaying its IPO to 2027 as it weighs a roughly $1 trillion valuation. In Tokyo, SoftBank tumbled about 12%, leading a rout in Asian tech that closed South Korea's Kospi down 5.8% after an 8% intraday drop tripped a circuit breaker.

The backdrop is a market on its back foot: on Thursday the Nasdaq Composite fell a fourth straight session — its first four-day losing streak since February — to 25,358.60, while the S&P 500 finished essentially flat at 7,357.49 and the Dow eked out a 0.14% gain to 51,920.62 after tagging a record intraday high. Friday's tape shows a clear, healthier rotation: health care is leading — Eli Lilly ≈ +6%, Johnson & Johnson ≈ +3%, AbbVie ≈ +2% — alongside industrials, materials and consumer staples (≈ +1%), even as megacaps slide, with Apple ≈ −6% (lineup-wide price hikes) and Microsoft ≈ −4% (an Xbox price increase) dragging Big Tech. Bucking the trend, Micron jumped on blockbuster memory earnings. Crude kept sliding — WTI near $71.50, around pre-war levels — while the 10-year Treasury yield eased toward 4.4%, slipping back below 4.5%. Markets will parse the University of Michigan's final June consumer-sentiment reading (consensus ~48.9, near historic lows) due after the open.
