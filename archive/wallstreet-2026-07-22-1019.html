The Lead

Stocks slip into a Big Tech earnings test as oil and tariffs bite — Super Micro rockets on a $60 billion order haul

US stocks were lower late Wednesday morning, extending an early pullback as a fresh climb in crude and new tariff threats offset a raft of morning earnings beats. As of roughly 10:15 a.m. ET the S&P 500 was down about 0.3%, the Nasdaq Composite off about 0.6% and the Dow lower by about 0.1%, with a closely watched semiconductor gauge sliding about 1.7% to lead the decline. Figures are point-in-time and will move through the session.

Two macro forces set the tone. Crude extended its climb — Brent rose about 2% to around $93 a barrel after briefly topping $95, its highest in over a month, and WTI added about 2% to around $86 — as an escalating US-Iran conflict stoked supply fears. And President Trump's move to impose fresh tariffs on Canada, with reports he is weighing new duties on dozens of countries, revived inflation worries. The 10-year Treasury yield stayed elevated near 4.6% after hitting 4.63% on Tuesday, its highest since mid-May.

Earnings cut both ways. The standout was Super Micro, which rocketed about 18% after disclosing more than $60 billion in new quarterly orders and a sharply raised gross-margin outlook. AT&T rose about 4% on a second-quarter beat, while GE Vernova fell about 4.5% despite blowout orders as investors fixed on cash conversion. The bigger test comes after the close: Alphabet and Tesla — the first two “Magnificent Seven” names to report this season — headline the docket, with IBM also due.

Note on precision: the precise index levels below are Tuesday's official closes, which reconcile to the cent — the S&P's +65.92, Nasdaq's +329.14 and Dow's +385.38 point changes all tie out. Today's editorial figures use percentage moves and are point-in-time while the session is open.

Movers & Drivers

▲ ~18%AI hardware

Super Micro rockets on a $60 billion order haul

Super Micro Computer surged about 18% — toward roughly $30 after a $25.50 Tuesday close — after disclosing more than $60 billion in new orders in its fiscal fourth quarter, a record backlog. It also lifted its gross-margin outlook to 15–17%, nearly double its prior 8.2–8.4% guidance. CEO Charles Liang tied the demand to fresh AI-infrastructure work, saying the company is co-building “another new Gigawatt AI datacenter” for SpaceX and xAI within a year. Soft revenue was overshadowed by the orders and margin reset.

▼ ~4.5%Earnings

GE Vernova falls despite blowout orders

GE Vernova posted second-quarter sales of $11.1 billion, up about 22% year over year and ahead of estimates, with organic orders jumping 88% to $24.2 billion and backlog climbing to $176.3 billion. It also raised its 2026 free-cash-flow outlook by about $5 billion at the midpoint. Yet shares fell roughly 4.5% as investors fixed on cash conversion — a "sell-the-news" reaction after a strong run into the print.

▲ ~4%Earnings

AT&T beats and adds subscribers

AT&T rose about 4% after a second-quarter beat. Adjusted EPS was $0.65, clearing the $0.59 consensus, and the company added 432,000 postpaid phone subscribers versus roughly 338,500 expected. Revenue of $31.56 billion (up about 2% year over year) came in a touch light against the ~$31.75 billion estimate — an EPS-and-subscriber beat with a slight top-line miss.

▲ ~1%Earnings

CME Group rises on a record first half

CME Group added about 1% after reporting earnings and revenue above expectations, with the exchange operator calling the first six months of 2026 its best-ever first half — a volatility-and-volume story as rate and commodity swings drive trading activity.

▼ ~0.5%Earnings

Philip Morris slips on soft guidance

Philip Morris International eased about 0.5% after issuing a weaker-than-expected third-quarter forecast, projecting earnings of $2.20 to $2.25 a share against analyst expectations near $2.42 — guidance that overshadowed the rest of the print.
