The Lead

Markets shut for the Fourth; Thursday's tape leaves the Dow at a record and the Nasdaq lower

U.S. equity and bond markets are closed all day Friday, July 3 in observance of Independence Day, so there is no trading session today — the last word came Thursday, and it split Wall Street. The Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at a record high, jumping 594.83 points (1.14%) to 52,900.07 — with an intraday peak of 52,903.85 — while a second straight session of semiconductor selling dragged the tech-heavy Nasdaq Composite down 0.80% (−207.36) to 25,832.67. The S&P 500 finished essentially flat at 7,483.24, rising less than a point as strength in blue-chip sectors offset the slide in technology.

The catalyst was a cooler-than-expected June jobs report that Wall Street read as easing pressure for another Fed rate hike. U.S. employers added just 57,000 positions in June — well short of the roughly 113,000 economists expected — and April and May were revised down by a combined 74,000. The unemployment rate ticked down to 4.2%, though largely because labor-force participation slipped to 61.5%, its lowest since March 2021. The Fed held its benchmark at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 with projections that had leaned toward a possible rate hike; after the soft print, futures cut the odds of a September hike to roughly 50%, down from about 67% before the data.

The drag again came from the AI-hardware trade: chip names sold off for a second straight session. The VanEck Semiconductor ETF fell about 4.5%, led by a 13.6% drop in Teradyne and an 11.5% slide in KLA, with AMD, Micron and Intel also lower and Tesla sinking. Money kept rotating toward blue chips, powering the Dow to its record even as the Nasdaq finished red. With markets dark for the long weekend, the next catalysts arrive next week; trading resumes Monday, July 6.
