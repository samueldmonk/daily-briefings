The Lead

Chips buckle, oil settles up more than 9% as the Hormuz blockade returns — S&P 500 closes down 0.79%, Nasdaq off 1.55%, Dow holds up best at −0.26%

Wall Street closed lower Monday after President Trump declared the U.S. is reinstating its naval blockade on Iranian shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and demanding a 20% "reimbursement" on all other cargo moving through the waterway, sending oil sharply higher and rattling chip and memory stocks worldwide (CNBC/Yahoo Finance/Axios). The S&P 500 fell 0.79% to finish at 7,515.34, the Nasdaq Composite dropped 1.55% to 25,873.18, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average slipped 138.37 points, or 0.26%, to 52,498.64 — cushioned by a jump in energy shares even as tech led the market lower (Motley Fool). Crude settled sharply higher on the reimposed blockade and fresh U.S.–Iran strikes: WTI futures climbed 9.4% to settle at $78.14 a barrel, the highest in nearly a month, while Brent rose 9.6% to settle at $83.30 (Bloomberg via Rigzone). The Joint Maritime Information Center said U.S. Central Command will begin enforcing the blockade of Iranian ports and coastal areas at 4 PM ET Tuesday (Rigzone/Axios).

Tech carried the pain: SK Hynix fell roughly 15% in Seoul — one of its largest single-day drops on record — after Korean brokerage KIS published a Q2 profit estimate 8% below consensus on slower HBM4 shipments, dragging the Kospi down about 9% and triggering a market-wide trading halt (24/7 Wall St/Al Jazeera). U.S.-listed chip and storage names followed: Nvidia fell 3.5% to $203.57, Micron and Western Digital each declined roughly 4–6%, and SpaceX slid more than 5% to a fresh post-IPO low (Motley Fool/CNBC). The weekend also brought a rare direct hit on energy infrastructure — Kuwait said an offshore drilling platform was struck and damaged — plus fresh Houthi missile and drone attacks on a Saudi airport (Rigzone/Bloomberg). The inflation stakes are immediate: oil's climb lands the day before Tuesday's June CPI print, Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony, and the unofficial kickoff of Q2 earnings season led by JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Bank of America, Citigroup and Wells Fargo (Yahoo Finance/Schwab).

Apple filed suit in the Northern District of California alleging the AI lab attempted to steal confidential information, naming two former Apple employees — Chang Liu and Tang Yew Tan — as defendants alongside OpenAI (Investrade/Hammerstone).

AI demand

TSMC Q2 revenue tops estimates on AI chips

Taiwan Semiconductor's Q2 revenue reached NT$1.27T (~$39.63B), up 36% year over year and ahead of the ~$39.31B consensus — with June sales alone at $13.8 billion, up 67.9% from a year ago — a fresh signal on AI demand ahead of its full report Thursday (Investrade/Hammerstone; 24/7 Wall St).

upgrades

American Express, Humana catch big upgrades

JPMorgan lifted American Express to Overweight with a $400 target, calling its premium warranted given defensive revenues; Wells Fargo raised Humana to Overweight with a $502 target ahead of managed-care Q2 reports (Investrade/Hammerstone).

AI buildout

Meta supersizes Louisiana AI campus to $50B

Meta now plans to invest as much as $50 billion in its Richland Parish, Louisiana data-center campus — targeting up to 5 gigawatts of compute — and is committing more than $1 billion for local roads, water and wastewater upgrades (24/7 Wall St). Morgan Stanley, meanwhile, now sees hyperscaler capex reaching $1.4 trillion in 2028.
