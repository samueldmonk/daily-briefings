The Lead

Hawkish hold sinks stocks: the Fed stays put but signals a 2026 hike, and Wall Street closes sharply lower

The Federal Reserve left its benchmark rate unchanged at 3.50–3.75% in a unanimous vote at the close of Chair Kevin Warsh's first meeting — a fourth straight hold, exactly as markets had priced (CME FedWatch had the odds well above 96%). But the message around the hold was sharply hawkish: the updated "dot plot" showed nine of 18 officials now projecting a rate hike later in 2026, the median year-end fed-funds estimate rose to ~3.8% from 3.4% in March's projections, the committee eliminated the single rate cut that had appeared in March, and it stripped its prior easing bias from the policy statement. Warsh confirmed at his debut press conference that he had refrained from submitting a dot of his own and said the Fed had "dropped" its forward guidance.

Stocks gyrated through the 2 p.m. decision, hit session lows during Warsh's press conference, and finished broadly lower. The S&P 500 and the Nasdaq Composite each closed down roughly 1% and the Dow Jones Industrial Average shed about 500 points, or roughly 1%, as traders fully priced in a quarter-point hike by year-end and rotated out of the megacaps — Microsoft, Meta, Alphabet and Amazon all finished red. Small caps held up better than the megacap-led decline, with the Russell 2000 the relative standout. The reaction underscored that, after a run of firmer inflation prints and the Iran-war energy shock, the burden of proof has shifted from "when do cuts start" to "could the next move be up."

Treasury yields jumped as the hawkish projections landed: the rate-sensitive 2-year yield rose about 11 basis points to roughly 4.15% and the 10-year climbed toward 4.47%, reversing the prior days' drift lower. The morning's data had already leaned hawkish: May retail sales rose 0.9% from April — well above the 0.5% expected and up 6.9% from a year earlier — with online sales up 1.5% and the core "control group" up 0.7%, a resilient-consumer read. The backdrop is a market that split sharply Tuesday, when the Dow set a fresh record close (51,999.67, +0.64%) as the only major index green while the S&P 500 (-0.08%), Nasdaq Composite (-1.15%) and Russell 2000 (-0.63%) slipped on a megacap-tech pullback. Crude, meanwhile, has kept falling: West Texas Intermediate is near $75 a barrel, around a three-month low, as tankers transit the Strait of Hormuz again following the U.S.–Iran de-escalation framework.

Megacap tech — red post-FOMC

The heavyweight tech names led the decline into the close: Microsoft, Meta Platforms, Alphabet and Amazon all finished lower as the hawkish dot plot and the jump in yields pressured the rate-sensitive growth complex.

Russell 2000 — relative standout

Small caps held up far better than the megacaps, with the Russell 2000 outperforming the S&P 500 and Nasdaq through the session even as the broad tape slid — a rare bright spot on an otherwise risk-off Fed day.

Rates & the dollar — yields surge

The clearest cross-asset signal of the day: short-term yields spiked as traders priced in a 2026 hike, with the 2-year up ~11bp to ~4.15% and the 10-year toward 4.47% — a hawkish repricing that did the damage to equities.
