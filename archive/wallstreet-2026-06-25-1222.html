The Lead

Dow notches a record intraday high on a value rotation as Micron's blowout lifts chips; PCE runs to a 3-year high

Wall Street advanced Thursday, June 25, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average touching a fresh all-time intraday high by around midday (about 12:15 p.m. ET). The move was driven less by Big Tech than by a rotation into the rest of the market: with several Magnificent Seven megacaps sinking, strength in industrials, health care and financials carried the indexes. As of around noon ET the S&P 500 was up about 0.5%, the Dow about 0.6% and the small-cap Russell 2000 about 0.4%, while the Nasdaq Composite lagged at about +0.2%, held back by the megacap-tech weakness. The spark was still chips: memory giant Micron's after-the-bell blowout the night before lifted the semiconductor complex, with SanDisk up about 14.7%. Micron crushed estimates with record fiscal-third-quarter revenue of about $41.46 billion (versus roughly $35.69 billion expected) and adjusted EPS of $25.11 (well above the ~$20.78 consensus) — revenue up about 346% from $9.3 billion a year earlier — and paired the print with a current-quarter sales forecast that topped Wall Street, reiterating that its HBM memory capacity is sold out through 2026 and that customers have committed about $22 billion to lock in supply. The stock rose about 12% in the regular session (after a ~15% after-hours pop) to a record high, with an 84.9% gross margin, its highest among major U.S. tech. This follows a mixed Wednesday close: the Dow rose 182.06 points (+0.35%) to 51,848.90 while the S&P 500 eased 0.10% to 7,358.22 and the Nasdaq Composite slipped 0.43% to 25,476.64.

The data slate was mixed. At 8:30 a.m. the Fed's preferred inflation gauge ran hot: May PCE rose 0.4% on the month and 4.1% year over year — the highest since April 2023; core PCE rose 0.3% on the month and 3.4% from a year ago, its highest since October 2023, with both readings broadly in line with forecasts. Separately, May durable-goods orders fell 4.5% (down $15.6 billion to $332.1 billion), a steeper drop than expected. Even so, the chip rally dominated: Qualcomm climbed about 10% after nearly doubling its fiscal-2029 non-handset revenue forecast to roughly $40 billion — powered by AI data-center infrastructure and a new deal with Meta — and memory and chip-equipment peers (SanDisk, Western Digital, Lam Research, KLA, Applied Materials) rose in sympathy. The macro backdrop stays unusual: the Fed held its target at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 and rate-path chatter lingers, while oil sits at pre-war lows (WTI dipped below $70 on Thursday, a fourth straight down session) and the 10-year Treasury yield hovers around 4.4%. Alphabet replaces Verizon in the Dow before the June 29 open.

Movers & Drivers

Micron (MU) ~+12%

The catalyst behind the chip bid. Record fiscal-Q3 revenue of about $41.46B (vs ~$35.69B expected), adjusted EPS of $25.11 (vs ~$20.78), an 84.9% gross margin (its highest among major U.S. tech), revenue up about 346% from a year ago, and a current-quarter forecast that topped estimates — with HBM sold out through 2026 and ~$22B of customer commitments. Shares rose about 12% in the regular session (after a ~15% after-hours jump) to a record high and reset sentiment on AI memory.

Qualcomm (QCOM) ~+10%

The chipmaker rallied about 10% after nearly doubling its projection for fiscal-2029 non-handset revenue to roughly $40 billion — fueled by AI data-center infrastructure and a new deal with Meta — reinforcing the AI growth story alongside Micron.

Bio-Techne (TECH) ~+19.6%

The day's biggest single mover — the life-sciences tools maker agreed to be acquired by Germany's Merck KGaA for $73 a share in cash, an enterprise value of about $11.3 billion and roughly a 36% premium to its one-month average price. Shares jumped about 20% toward the deal price; the transaction is expected to close in late 2026 or early 2027.

Laggards & the meme cooldown lower

Several megacaps weighed on the Nasdaq: Apple (AAPL) fell about 4% after raising prices on select Macs — the new MacBook Neo starts at $699 (up from $599) and the M3 Ultra Mac Studio jumps $1,300 to $5,299 — citing higher component costs as AI demand lifts memory and storage prices. Dell Technologies (DELL) fell about 6.7% and Albemarle (ALB) lost about 4.4%. Wendy's (WEN) gave back about 3.4% to roughly $7.60, cooling a day after a meme-driven ~26% surge.
