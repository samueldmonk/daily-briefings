Top Story

No patch yet: Cisco warns of an actively exploited root-level flaw in Catalyst SD-WAN Manager

Cisco has disclosed that CVE-2026-20245, a high-severity vulnerability in the CLI of Cisco Catalyst SD-WAN Manager (formerly SD-WAN vManage), is being exploited in the wild — and there is no patch or mitigation available yet. The flaw (CVSS 7.8) stems from insufficient validation of user-supplied input and lets an authenticated local attacker with netadmin privileges upload a crafted file to execute arbitrary commands as root.

Cisco said it has observed limited cases where exploitation resulted in a configuration change being pushed down to edge devices. The disclosure follows CVE-2026-20182, a maximum-severity (CVSS 10.0) authentication-bypass bug in the same product that was previously exploited as a zero-day to gain administrative access. Defenders running SD-WAN Manager should tighten access controls and watch for unexpected configuration pushes while a fix is pending.

Breaches & Incidents
BREACH

Aflac: data on 22M+ people stolen

Insurance giant Aflac confirmed a June cyberattack exposed personal information of more than 22 million customers, employees, agents and others — including names, contact details, claims and health information, and Social Security numbers. Affected individuals are being offered two years of identity protection.

BREACH

DentaQuest: 2.6M accounts, ShinyHunters leak

Dental benefits administrator DentaQuest confirmed on June 2 that its network was breached. Extortion group ShinyHunters listed the company and claimed more than 234 GB of stolen data; reporting puts the exposure at roughly 2.6 million accounts.

FRAUD

World Cup 2026 fraud wave

Researchers and the FBI are warning of a surge of FIFA-themed fraud targeting World Cup 2026 fans — thousands of lookalike FIFA domains, banking malware hidden inside pirate streaming apps, and operations cloning FIFA's login page.

SUPPLY CHAIN

Hola Browser laced with a crypto miner

The Windows version of the Hola Browser was compromised in a supply-chain attack that delivered an undeclared executable identified by researchers as a cryptocurrency miner — the latest reminder that trusted software updates remain a soft target.
