The Lead

Chip-led rebound holds into the afternoon — Wall Street claws back part of Friday's rout even as Mideast strikes keep oil and yields elevated (as of ~3 PM ET)

U.S. stocks extended their bounce into Monday afternoon, recovering some of last week's brutal slide as the semiconductor selloff halted. As of about 3 p.m. ET, the S&P 500 was up roughly 0.9%, the Nasdaq Composite climbed around 1.4%, and the Dow added about 0.6%; the small-cap Russell 2000 joined the advance rather than lagging it. Around half the S&P's members were higher, led by the technology sector, which advanced more than 3%. Stocks firmed on signs of a cautious de-escalation in the Middle East paired with a halt to the chip rout — Iran said it had ended its military operation against Israel after weekend strikes and President Trump signaled optimism on a deal with Tehran, though fresh Iran–Israel missile exchanges left a proposed 60-day ceasefire looking fragile.

Semiconductors led the recovery. Marvell Technology jumped (roughly 8.8% intraday, after gaining nearly 9% premarket) on confirmation it will join the S&P 500 effective June 22; Micron rose about 7%, Nvidia added around 2%, and fellow incoming index member Flex climbed about 4%. Corning surged more than 9% after Amazon announced a multibillion-dollar optical-fiber agreement to power its U.S. data centers. The bounce follows Friday's rout, when the Nasdaq Composite plunged 4.18% — its worst day since April 2025 — and a chip selloff erased roughly $1 trillion in value after a hot May jobs report (+172k vs ~80k expected) fueled Fed-hike bets. Capping the rally, WTI crude jumped more than 4% above $94 a barrel on the Iran–Israel strikes, while the 10-year Treasury yield held near 4.57%, a two-week high. Citigroup, meanwhile, lifted its year-end S&P 500 target to 8,100, citing AI-driven earnings strength.
