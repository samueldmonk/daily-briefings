The Lead

Chip stocks lead a rebound as Middle East fears cool — but small caps, oil and a 2-week-high yield keep the bounce in check (as of ~1 PM ET)

Wall Street climbed Monday, clawing back part of last week's brutal slide as the semiconductor selloff halted and Middle East tensions eased. As of early afternoon (~1 p.m. ET), the S&P 500 was up about 0.7% (roughly +55 points), the Nasdaq Composite rose around 1.35% (about +390 points), and the Dow added roughly 0.3% (about +160 points) — paring slightly from a firmer open. The advance was uneven: the small-cap Russell 2000 lagged badly, down about 3.5%, as a jump in Treasury yields weighed on rate-sensitive names. Stocks firmed after Iran said it had ended its military operation against Israel following weekend strikes, and President Trump signaled optimism on a deal with Tehran — easing fears of a wider escalation.

Semiconductors led the recovery. Marvell Technology jumped (about 9% premarket and roughly 8.8% intraday, per Investing.com) after being named a new S&P 500 member effective June 22; Intel surged about 10% (to ~$109) on reports Alphabet ordered more than three million tensor processing units for 2028; and Corning rose more than 9% (to ~$194) on a multibillion-dollar Amazon optical-fiber deal for data centers. Nvidia added about 2% and Micron roughly 7%. The bounce follows Friday's rout, when the Nasdaq Composite plunged 4.18% — its worst day since April 2025 — and a chip selloff erased roughly $1 trillion in value after a hot May jobs report (+172k vs ~80k expected) fueled Fed-hike bets. Capping the rally: WTI crude jumped more than 4% above $94 a barrel intraday on the Israel–Iran strikes before easing back toward $91.40, while the 10-year Treasury yield climbed to about 4.57%, a two-week high. Apple opened its Worldwide Developers Conference today, with investors watching for AI announcements; Citigroup separately lifted its year-end S&P 500 target to 8,100.
