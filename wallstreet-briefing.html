The Lead

Stocks open lower as US–Iran strikes lift crude and a hot-but-mixed May CPI keeps the Fed in focus — as of ~10 AM ET Wednesday, June 10

U.S. stocks slipped in early trading after the United States and Iran traded fresh military strikes overnight, reviving fears of a wider Middle East conflict and pushing oil higher. As of roughly 10 AM ET, the S&P 500 was down about 0.48%, the Dow Jones Industrial Average off about 0.59%, and the Nasdaq Composite lower by about 0.62%, while the small-cap Russell 2000 bucked the trend with a gain of roughly 0.41% — a continuation of the rotation that has favored small caps over crowded megacap tech. The U.S. launched new strikes on Iran after President Trump said Tehran had shot down an American military helicopter patrolling the Strait of Hormuz; Trump warned that Iran was "taking too long to negotiate a deal" and "now they will have to pay the price."

The session followed a May Consumer Price Index report that ran hot on the headline but offered relief at the core. Headline CPI rose 0.5% on the month and 4.2% from a year earlier — above 4% for the first time in three years and the highest annual rate since April 2023 — while core CPI (ex-food and energy) rose just 0.2% month over month, below the 0.3% estimate, for a 2.9% annual rate. Energy did the damage: energy prices accounted for more than 60% of the monthly increase, with gasoline up about 40.5% from a year ago, reflecting the supply shock from the Iran war and disruption around the Strait of Hormuz. The hot-but-mixed print keeps the Federal Reserve squarely in focus before its June 16–17 meeting under new Chair Kevin Warsh, with a hold at 3.50–3.75% still widely expected even as a minority of traders price rising odds of a hike later this year. All eyes turn next to Oracle, which reports fiscal Q4 results after Wednesday's close. (Closing levels are in the Weekly Scorecard below; live quotes stream in the widgets above.)
