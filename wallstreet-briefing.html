The Lead

Nasdaq plunges 4% — biggest drop in over a year — as hot jobs report and chip rout end the S&P 500's 9-week streak

Friday's session ended in a broad rout. The Nasdaq Composite lost about 4% — its biggest decline since the tariff turmoil of early 2025 — the S&P 500 fell roughly 2.6%, and the Dow dropped about 697 points, around 1.4%, per CNBC and TheStreet (figures as of the 4 PM ET close; official levels pending verification). TheStreet reports the semiconductor slide wiped roughly $1 trillion from markets.

The trigger was a much stronger-than-expected May employment report: payrolls grew by 172,000 — more than double the ~80,000 economists expected — with unemployment unchanged at 4.3%. In a classic "good news is bad news" reaction, the print fueled bets that the Federal Reserve's next move could be a hike rather than a cut, sending the 10-year Treasury yield up to its highest level since May 21. Chip stocks, already wobbling after Broadcom's Wednesday-night earnings failed to raise its AI outlook, sold off violently for a second straight day. With Friday's losses, the S&P 500 logged its first negative week in 10, down more than 2%, while the Nasdaq lost roughly 4% on the week.

Movers & Drivers
CHIPS / AI

Semis crater; ~$1T wiped out ▼

The sell-off deepened all session: by midday Micron was down 12.2%, AMD 10.6%, Broadcom 7.4% and Nvidia 5.8%, per The Motley Fool; Nvidia ended near 6% lower as the Nasdaq closed down 4%, per Yahoo Finance. Marvell lost 8% in early trading.

EARNINGS

Broadcom's miss keeps stinging ▼

Broadcom posted record Q2 revenue of $22.19B but narrowly missed analyst estimates (~$22.27B expected), with a software miss and an unchanged AI outlook. Its failure to raise the AI chip outlook is cited as a key trigger of the two-day sector rout.

RATES

Yields jump on jobs surprise ▼

The 10-year Treasury yield rose 5 basis points to 4.534%, its highest since May 21, and the 30-year popped 5 bps to 5.021%, per CNBC, after May payrolls topped the 80,000 consensus by more than double. Traders shifted toward pricing a possible Fed hike this year.
