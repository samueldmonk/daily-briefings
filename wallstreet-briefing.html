The Lead

Stocks slide as US–Iran tensions flare and the chip rotation resumes — Nasdaq off more than 2.5% as of ~1 PM ET, with the Dow holding up best

Wall Street's two-day bounce gave way to a broad sell-off Tuesday afternoon as renewed US–Iran tensions met a fresh rotation out of crowded technology names. As of about 1 PM ET, the Nasdaq Composite was down roughly 2.5%, the S&P 500 off about 1.5%, and the more defensive Dow Jones held up best, slipping about 0.75% (around 380 points). The trigger: President Trump said the US "must respond" to Iran's latest retaliation, reviving worries that a move could reignite the regional conflict. Chipmakers Micron, Nvidia and Broadcom reversed earlier gains and turned lower, leading the tape down. (Figures are intraday moves as of ~1 PM ET and will change through the session; Tuesday's official close posts after 4 PM ET.)

Breadth was less grim than the headline indices: more than 1,300 NYSE issues were advancing against roughly 550 declining, and small caps bucked the trend — the Russell 2000 clung to a modest gain of about half a percent as money rotated toward rate-sensitive corners. Homebuilders led the way, with the SPDR S&P Homebuilders ETF (XHB) up about 2.5% and names like LGI Homes (+5.2%) and Century Communities (+4.7%) climbing as a roughly 2.3% drop in oil eased inflation expectations. Traders are bracing for Wednesday's May CPI (8:30 AM ET, June 10), with consensus near 4.2–4.3% year over year on the headline; a hot print would harden the case that the Fed could be forced to hike later this year. The backdrop: Monday's session was a chip-led rebound, with the Nasdaq Composite closing up 0.86% at 25,929.66 and the S&P 500 up 0.30% at 7,405.73, while the Dow slipped 0.16% to 50,786.01 — a partial recovery from Friday's rout, when the Nasdaq Composite fell 4.18% (its worst day since April 2025) as a chip selloff erased roughly $1 trillion after a hot May jobs report.
